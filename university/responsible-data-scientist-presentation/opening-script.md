# Opening Script: "How to Be a Responsible Data Scientist"

A tailored opener for your Data Visualization class talk. Grounded in the [presentation-playbook](../../references/presentation-playbook.md) openings research. The principle: **hook first, name and agenda later.** Earn attention in the first 8 seconds, then introduce yourself around the 60 to 90 second mark.

---

## Recommended hook: "Two charts, same data" (prop / demonstration)

This is the strongest option because it *dramatizes your entire thesis in 20 seconds* and flows straight into the talk. It uses a prop-style visual reveal plus a question, two of the most effective hook types.

**Setup (build this as your opening slide, or slide 0 before the title):** two line or bar charts side by side, no title yet.
- Left chart: a steep, dramatic-looking rise (truncated y-axis, starts at, say, 90 instead of 0).
- Right chart: the same numbers plotted honestly from a zero baseline, so the rise looks modest.
- Do not label which is which yet.

### Word-for-word script (about 75 to 90 seconds)

> *(Say nothing for 2 seconds. Let them look at the two charts.)*
>
> "Take a look at these two charts. One company shows the board the chart on the left, and they approve a huge budget increase. Another shows the board the chart on the right, and they say, let's wait."
>
> *(Pause 3 seconds. Make eye contact with one person.)*
>
> "Here's the thing. **It's the exact same data.** Same numbers, same company, same quarter. The only difference is where the y-axis starts."
>
> *(Pause. Let it land.)*
>
> "So which chart is lying? ... Actually, neither one is technically false. And that's the problem. You can tell the truth with numbers and still completely mislead the people who trust you."
>
> *(Beat.)*
>
> "I'm Aleem, and today I want to talk about how *not* to be the person who makes that left-hand chart. How to be a responsible data scientist, someone people can actually trust with the numbers that drive real decisions."
>
> *(Transition into the deck.)*
>
> "Because being responsible isn't just one thing. It runs across six areas, from how you collect the data all the way to the final chart on the screen."
>
> **-> Advance to Slide 2 / 3 (the pillars).**

### Delivery notes
- The power is in the **reveal + the pause**. Do not rush "it's the exact same data." Say it slowly, then stop. Silence does the work.
- Keep your hands in the gesture zone; gesture left and right to physically separate the two charts.
- Hold eye contact on one friendly face through the "which chart is lying?" question. It calms your nerves and pulls the room in.
- This hook ties directly to your **Slide 7 (Honest Visualization, Part 1)**. Call back to it there: "Remember the two charts from the start? This is exactly that, the truncated axis."
- **Bookend it at the close:** end the talk by returning to this image. "The two charts from the beginning were the same data. Your job is to make sure the one you show is the honest one."

## Alternative hook A: Surprising stat + audience prompt

Good if you want lower setup and more audience interaction.

> "Quick show of hands: who here has ever looked at a chart in the news, or an ad, and later found out it was misleading?"
>
> *(Wait for hands. Most will go up.)*
>
> "Almost everyone. Now keep your hand up if you'd be able to spot exactly *how* it was misleading. ... A lot fewer hands. That gap, between feeling fooled and knowing how, is what separates a responsible data scientist from everyone else. Let me show you what's in that gap."

Why it works: a question forces engagement, and the hands make the problem feel real and personal before you've shown a single slide.

## Alternative hook B: The bold claim

Good if you want to open with energy and confidence.

> "Most people think the way you lie with data is to make up numbers. That's amateur hour. The real damage gets done with numbers that are 100% accurate, and a chart that's designed to fool you. By the end of this talk, you'll be able to spot it, and more importantly, you'll know how to never be the one doing it."

Why it works: it flips conventional wisdom (lying = fake numbers) and sets up clear stakes. Back it immediately with the two-charts visual.

---

## Openers to avoid for this talk
- Do not open with "Hi, I'm Aleem, my topic today is responsible data science, here's my agenda." It flattens the energy before you start.
- Do not apologize for nerves, slide quality, or time. Walk in and hit the hook.
- Save "what is a data scientist" definitions for after the hook, if at all.
