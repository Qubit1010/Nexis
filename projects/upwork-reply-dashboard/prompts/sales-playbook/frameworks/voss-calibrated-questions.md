# Voss Framework — Calibrated Questions, Labels, No-Oriented Closes

> **Source basis:** All 15 calibrated questions, 9 labels, and 4 no-oriented questions below are sourced directly from `references/research-synthesis.md` (Q4, Q5 sections) which synthesizes Black Swan Group and NeverSplitTheDifference.com material along with Voss applications documented in Cole Gordon's 8-step framework, Sandler's pain funnel, and Hormozi's value-equation calls. Citations are the source indices from the NotebookLM research.

## Why this file

Voss's framework is Aleem's existing foundation — the LinkedIn/IG DM responder skills already use labels and no-oriented questions. This file is the **canonical question and label bank** all other scripts (live conversation playbook, discovery call, objection riffs) pull from.

The principle:
- **Calibrated questions** (start with "What" or "How") force the prospect to do the cognitive work and gives them the illusion of control while you steer.
- **Labels** ("It seems like..." / "It sounds like...") bypass the prospect's defensive filter and trigger floodgates of truth.
- **No-oriented questions** make the prospect feel safe and protected, paradoxically raising commitment vs. yes-oriented asks.

## The 15 calibrated questions (use in discovery calls, live DMs)

All sourced from research synthesis Q4. Each question has a primary use case.

### Pain-discovery (use early in conversation)

1. **"What is the core issue here?"**
   *Use:* When prospect has surface-complained but you need the root.

2. **"What is the biggest challenge you face?"**
   *Use:* Opener-stage discovery — broader than pain-funnel-step-1.

3. **"What makes this urgent?"**
   *Use:* Before pitching anything. If they can't articulate urgency, you don't have a deal.

4. **"How have you handled these issues in the past?"**
   *Use:* Surfaces previous failed attempts (and creates space for your "Mistake ABC" pivot — see `frameworks/objection-riffs.md`).

5. **"How does that affect things?"**
   *Use:* Follow-up to ANY pain statement. Forces the prospect to quantify the implication themselves.

### Cost / consequence questions (Sandler-style amplification)

6. **"What happens if you do nothing?"**
   *Use:* Right after the pain is surfaced. Converts a "nice to have" into a "must fix."

7. **"What position are you in if this doesn't change?"**
   *Use:* Personal-stakes version of #6. Better for founders.

8. **"What happens if we fail?"**
   *Use:* Late-stage — surfaces hidden risks they haven't told you about.

9. **"How will this look in a year if things don't change?"**
   *Use:* Time-horizon framing. Powerful for founders thinking quarterly.

10. **"How long are you willing to wait?"**
    *Use:* Closes the "we'll think about it" loop. Makes them name a deadline.

### Solution-framing questions

11. **"What are you trying to accomplish by doing that?"**
    *Use:* When they describe their current approach. Surfaces gaps in their reasoning.

12. **"How are you trying to accomplish this by doing it that way?"**
    *Use:* Variant of #11 — surfaces method-vs-outcome mismatch.

13. **"How is that worthwhile?"**
    *Use:* When prospect describes a current solution that isn't working. Forces them to defend it (and they can't).

14. **"How does this fit your timeline?"**
    *Use:* Pre-close — turns abstract interest into a specific commitment window.

### Negotiation-pressure question

15. **"How am I supposed to do that?"** ⭐
    *Use:* When prospect demands a discount, pushes for free work, or asks for terms you can't meet. Delivered with curious tone (not sarcastic). Forces them to solve YOUR problem. Voss's signature line.

## The 9 documented labels

Labels begin with **"It seems like..."** or **"It sounds like..."** or **"Seems like..."** — never with "I think" or "I feel."

| Label | When to use it |
|---|---|
| **"It seems like you have some concerns here"** | Prospect has objections or negative emotions. Beats asking "what objections do you have?" |
| **"It seems like you are frustrated with me"** | De-escalates conflict in stalled negotiation. |
| **"It seems like you are under a massive amount of pressure"** | Surfaces "black swans" — hidden deadlines, exits, internal politics. |
| **"Seems like you've done a lot of research already"** | Use when a prospect demands pricing but resists discovery. Tests for fake opportunity. |
| **"It seems like there is more on your mind"** | Use when prospect says "you're right" or tries to brush you off. Pivots deeper. |
| **"It seems like this could save you time"** | Positive-frame label. Use after they describe a manual pain. |
| **"It seems like integration's a worry"** | Proactively addresses an objection before they raise it. |
| **"Seems like you saw some things you liked"** | After demo / proof. Triggers a floodgate of details. |
| **"Seems like you've given this a lot of thought"** | Flatters and probes. Gets them to inadvertently reveal context. |

**How to use a label (the move):**
1. State the label.
2. **Shut up.** Stay silent for 3-5 full seconds.
3. Let the prospect fill the silence — they almost always do, and they almost always reveal more than they meant to.

If they don't fill it, follow with one of the calibrated questions above.

## The 4 documented no-oriented questions

Voss's principle: "Yes is nothing without how." Saying "yes" feels like commitment / loss of control — most people resist. "No" feels safe and protective — they say it more easily, and you can build from there.

Use these to invert your asks:

1. **"Is now a bad time to talk?"** (or **"Is now a bad time to focus on this?"**)
   *Use:* Opening a discovery call. Replaces "Got a few minutes?" Documented in Cole Gordon's UFC frame.

2. **"Does this seem like a bad idea?"**
   *Use:* The primary close. Replaces "Want to move forward?" If they say "no, it doesn't seem like a bad idea" — you have a yes.

3. **"Would it offend you if I shared some solutions?"**
   *Use:* Pre-demo / pre-pitch. Earns permission without asking for permission.

4. **"Would it offend you if I asked a few quick questions to align our implementation timeline?"**
   *Use:* When transitioning from close to next steps. Documented by Cole Gordon as the soft handoff to logistics.

**Pattern for inventing your own no-oriented question:**
- Old (yes-oriented): "Want to schedule a call?"
- New (no-oriented): "Is it a bad idea to grab 20 min next week?"
- Old: "Are you interested?"
- New: "Have you given up on solving this?"
- Old: "Would you like to move forward?"
- New: "Does this seem like a bad idea?"

## Combining the three (the canonical Voss move on a sales call)

The full Voss play, in order:

```
1. Label what you're observing
   "It seems like you're under a lot of pressure with this onboarding bottleneck."

2. [SILENCE — let them fill it]
   → Prospect reveals more pain than they planned to.

3. Calibrated question to deepen
   "How does that affect the team's ability to onboard at scale?"

4. [SILENCE]
   → Prospect quantifies the cost themselves.

5. Another label to mirror what they just said
   "Sounds like this is becoming a ceiling on the whole business."

6. [SILENCE]
   → Prospect confirms; commits emotionally to fixing it.

7. No-oriented question to advance
   "Would it be a bad idea to look at what we built for [peer] who had the same issue?"
```

This sequence repeats with new pains. Each loop deepens the buy-in. By the time you present a solution, the prospect has already articulated the value to themselves three times.

## Voss principles (the meta)

From the research synthesis (Q4, Q5):

1. **Tactical empathy beats logic.** Never argue with a feeling. Label it first.
2. **The most powerful word in negotiation is "no" (not "yes").** Build asks that invite "no."
3. **Calibrated questions transfer effort.** Make THEM solve your problem.
4. **Silence is a tactic.** Most people lose negotiations because they fill silence.
5. **Mirror the last 3 words.** When you don't know what to say next, repeat the prospect's last 3 words as a question. Triggers them to expand.
