# Objection Riffs — 10 Cited Responses

> **Source basis:** Every response below is sourced from `references/research-synthesis.md` (Q5 section) which combines Voss (Black Swan Group), Sandler, Cole Gordon, Becc Holland, Frank Kern, and Hormozi as documented in the NotebookLM research. Where a source doesn't have a documented response, I say "not in sources" — Aleem fills with his own judgment.

## How to use this file

When a prospect raises an objection in a DM or on a call:

1. **Don't argue or defend.** Voss's principle: never argue with a feeling.
2. **Label first** (acknowledge the feeling). Look up the right label in `frameworks/voss-calibrated-questions.md`.
3. **Then use the riff below.**
4. **Then go silent.** Wait.

The riffs are starters, not scripts. Match tone to the prospect.

## The 10 most-common objections (with cited responses)

### 1. "Too expensive" / "It's too much money"

**Voss's response (calibrated question):**
> "How am I supposed to do that?"

Delivered with **curious tone, not sarcastic**. Forces them to solve your problem. Often gets them to either name a workable number themselves OR back off the objection (because they realize it's not really about money).

**Cole Gordon's structural response (mental math on the spot):**
1. Quickly evaluate their actual financial parameters
2. Offer the highest viable tier first (pay-in-full / annual)
3. If they push back, drop to two-pay or three-pay to manage fear — *don't drop the price, drop the cadence*

**Hormozi reframe (anchor on cost of inaction):**
> "You said this is costing you ~$[X]/month in lost time. Our fee is $[Y]. It pays for itself in [N] weeks. After that, every month is found money. What does it cost you to leave it broken for 3 more months?"

---

### 2. "I need to think about it"

**Not directly in sources** (no documented Voss/Sandler response specifically for this phrase).

**Synthesis-based suggestion** (extrapolating from Voss labels + Cole Gordon's framework):

Voss label first:
> "It seems like there's something specific that's not quite landing yet."

[Silence. Wait.]

Then if they don't fill it:
> "Most people who say that are usually circling one of three things — is it the price, the timing, or whether this'll actually work for you? Which one's closest?"

(This forces them to name the real objection, which you can then handle from the bank below.)

---

### 3. "I need to talk to my partner / team / wife / co-founder"

**Cole Gordon's "double tie-down":**
> "Hey, no problem. That aside, how do you feel about the process? Do you feel like what we covered is essentially what you need to [solve the specific problem we identified]?"

[Wait for affirmative.]

> "Got it. So, setting the [partner/co-founder] approval aside for a moment, it sounds like you are completely aligned. If we can structure this so there is no financial risk to the organization, are you ready to get started?"

This isolates UNCERTAINTY (the real objection) from LOGISTICS (the stated one). If they're uncertain, the "I need to talk to them" line was cover. If they're aligned, you can offer the risk-reversal close and proceed.

---

### 4. "We're already working with someone" / "We have a vendor"

**Becc Holland's response (DO NOT criticize the competitor):**
> Praise the existing setup, then highlight your differences.

Example:
> "Smart — [Vendor X] is solid at [what they do well]. The reason I'm still reaching out: most teams using them eventually hit [specific limit]. We come in as the [specific complementary piece]. Worth a 15-min look just to see if there's a gap?"

**Sandler's Negative Reverse Selling (alternative):**
> "It sounds like you might already have a solution in place — is this even worth exploring?"

(Reverse psychology: by giving them permission to disqualify, they often defend continuing.)

---

### 5. "We're not the right fit"

**Not in sources.** No documented response.

**Synthesis-based:**
Voss label:
> "It seems like something specific is making you say that — what's the part that doesn't fit?"

If their reason is real, thank them and disqualify cleanly (don't push). If their reason is vague, you might be hitting a brush-off (handle as #6).

---

### 6. "Send me more info / email me" (the brush-off)

**Sandler's "Reversing":** Reply with a gentle question to find the intent behind the brush-off rather than blindly sending material.

(Exact phrasing not documented in sources, so here's a synthesis-based version:)

> "Happy to. So I send the right thing — what specifically would you want it to answer? Is it more 'does this work for [their setup]' or 'what does it actually cost'?"

This forces them to either (a) name the real concern, or (b) admit the brush-off and self-disqualify. Either way you save the follow-up email from going into a black hole.

---

### 7. "Not a priority right now" / "Bad timing"

**Sandler pattern interrupt:**
> "It seems like this may have dropped in priority — should we pause for now or revisit later?"

This is no-oriented. "Pause for now" feels safe to say yes to. But it also gets them to name a specific revisit window — converting an indefinite no into a specific calendar entry.

**Follow-up calibrated question if they say "revisit later":**
> "When you say later — what's the specific event that would make this become a priority? A new hire? A revenue milestone? Something else?"

---

### 8. "We tried something like this before and it didn't work"

**Cole Gordon's "Mistake ABC" framework:**

Validate the past attempt, name the specific failure pattern, then differentiate.

> "Totally fair. One of the biggest mistakes people make when they try [your category] is [the common mistake — e.g., 'they build the automation in a tool the team won't actually use']. The problem with that is [the consequence — e.g., 'so it sits unused, and the manual work creeps back in within a month']. Instead, what we do is [your specific approach — e.g., 'we build inside the tool your team is already in, so adoption is zero-friction']. That's how we kept [Peer] running [X] for 6 months without it slipping."

Three steps: name the mistake → name the consequence → name your specific differentiator.

---

### 9. "What if it doesn't work?" / "No guarantee?"

**Frank Kern's documented risk reversal:**
> "If you feel I wasted your time, I will send you a check for $1,500."

**Aleem-adapted version:**
> "If we don't take at least 5 hrs/week off your plate inside 21 days, full refund. You keep what we built. The only risk you take is the 45 minutes of onboarding."

**Hormozi-stack version (multiple guarantees layered):**
- Time guarantee: "Built and live in 14 days or you don't pay."
- Result guarantee: "Saves 5+ hrs/week or full refund."
- Workmanship guarantee: "We monitor and fix it for 30 days post-deploy at no extra cost."

The more risk you reverse, the less Perceived Likelihood becomes a blocker.

---

### 10. "Just curious, how much does it cost?" (asked early in call, before discovery)

**Sandler's reverse question:**
> "That's a fair question. Usually our clients find the budget once they're fully convinced of the value. Are we at that point yet?"

(This gets you back into discovery — which is where the deal actually gets won.)

**Voss's label (alternative for prospects pushing hard for price while resisting discovery):**
> "Seems like you've done a lot of research already."

This is the **"fake opportunity test"** — if they're price-shopping and avoiding qualification, they're not a real prospect. Voss's label exposes that. Real prospects say "yeah, doing my homework, what's the range?" and you can proceed. Fake prospects fold or disengage.

## Voss's universal objection move (use when no specific riff fits)

The "How am I supposed to do that?" calibrated question (objection #1 above) works for almost any pushback because it shifts the burden of problem-solving back to the prospect WHILE letting them maintain illusion of control.

Examples:
- "Your timeline doesn't work for us." → "How am I supposed to do that?"
- "Can you cut 30% off?" → "How am I supposed to do that?"
- "I need it next week." → "How am I supposed to do that?"

Delivered with curious tone (never sarcastic), the prospect often answers their own objection — or quietly withdraws it.

## Sandler's distinction: Objection vs. Condition

**Not explicitly in our sources** as named by Sandler, but Cole Gordon's framework distinguishes:

- **Uncertainty objections** — emotional, fixable. ("Will this work for us?" / "I'm worried about the integration.") → Handle with proof, risk reversal, labels.
- **Logistical objections** — structural, sometimes real. ("We have a hiring freeze for 6 months.") → Acknowledge, qualify whether it's real or cover, and decide whether to push or park.

When you hear an objection: ask yourself "is this fixable today?" If yes — handle. If no — park it for a calendared follow-up.
