# Welcome Email Voice Guide

The welcome email is the first official client-facing communication after the deal is
signed. It sets the tone for the engagement: confident, organized, warm, and specific.

## Hard rules

- **No em dashes.** Use commas or periods. Em dashes corrupt cp1252-encoded copies pasted
  back into Docs (see Memory: `feedback_google_docs_encoding.md`).
- **No emojis.** Ever. Even friendly ones.
- **No "I hope this finds you well."** Banned. It signals filler.
- **Under 200 words** in the body. Aleem will read every draft before it sends. Long emails
  feel like work; short ones feel like care.
- **First-person plural ("we", "our team")** when describing what NexusPoint will do, not
  first-person singular. Aleem represents the agency here.
- **Sign off as `Aleem`** unless the user says otherwise. Plain name. No title block.

## Voice and structure

The email has 5 beats. Hit each in 1-3 sentences. Don't add extra beats.

### 1. Opening — mirror something specific

Reference something concrete from the proposal or call. Not "great chatting with you"
(generic). Closer to "thanks for the trust on the X rebuild" or "good call yesterday on
the booking flow." This is tactical empathy: show you were paying attention.

### 2. What's already set up

Tell them concretely what exists now: the Drive folder, the onboarding doc, the project
checklist Sheet. Include the links. This signals competence without bragging about it.

### 3. What we need from them

Bulleted list of the 2-4 highest-leverage things you need to start. Pulled from the
"What we need from you" section of the onboarding doc. Don't ask for everything; ask for
what unblocks day 1. The rest can come later.

Examples by project type:
- Web/CMS: brand assets (logo, fonts, colors), copy or content references, login access
  to existing site
- Full-stack: GitHub access, hosting access, third-party API keys, sample data
- AI automation: access to the tools being automated, sample inputs, error edge cases

### 4. The kickoff call

Propose a kickoff call. Either offer 2-3 specific time windows (Aleem's PKT timezone) or
ask them to pick from a calendar link. Short and direct.

### 5. Signoff

One closing sentence that lands warmly without being saccharine. Examples:
- "Excited to dig in."
- "Looking forward to building this."
- "Glad to have you on board."

Then `Aleem` on a new line.

---

## Subject line formula

`Welcome to NexusPoint, [Project name] kickoff`

Or, if the project name is long or generic, swap to:
`[Client name] x NexusPoint, kickoff and next steps`

Avoid "Re:" or fake-thread openers. This is a fresh email, not a reply.

## Example (web design project)

**Subject:** Welcome to NexusPoint, Acme website rebuild kickoff

Hi Sarah,

Thanks for the trust on the Acme rebuild. The team is locked in and ready to go.

Everything for the project lives in one place now:

- Project folder: [link to client folder]
- Onboarding doc: [link to onboarding doc]
- Checklist: [link to checklist sheet]

To start design this week, we need three things from you:

- Logo files in vector format (SVG or AI)
- 1-2 reference sites you like (style, not features)
- Any existing brand colors or fonts, if you have them

Could you do a 45-minute kickoff call this week? I have Wednesday or Thursday afternoon
free, your timezone. Or if it's easier, drop a slot here: [calendar link].

Excited to dig in.

Aleem

## What to avoid

- Buzzwords: "synergy", "leverage", "ecosystem", "world-class", "cutting-edge"
- Hedging: "I think we might be able to", "we'll try our best"
- Over-promising: don't commit to specific dates the proposal didn't already commit to
- Salesy closing: "Don't hesitate to reach out!" — they know they can email you
- Pricing or scope re-litigation: that's done. Move forward.
