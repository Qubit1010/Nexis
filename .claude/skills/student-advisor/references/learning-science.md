# Learning Science — The Scoreboard

What actually works, ranked by the evidence. Lead with this on any study-technique question. Full
citations in `research-synthesis.md` Q1 (and Q4 for retention). The one-line takeaway: **test yourself,
space it out, stop rereading.**

## The ranking (from the meta-analyses)

| Technique | Evidence | Verdict |
|---|---|---|
| **Practice testing / retrieval practice** | Testing effect g ≈ 0.50-0.63; "high utility" in Dunlosky 2013; top-2 in Donoghue & Hattie (242 studies) | **Do this first.** Self-quiz, flashcards, blank-page recall |
| **Distributed / spaced practice** | Spacing effect up to d > 1.0; "high utility"; top-2 in the meta-analysis | **Do this too.** Spread study across days/weeks |
| **Interleaving** | Helps discrimination and transfer (mixing problem types) | Strong, especially for problem-solving subjects |
| **Dual coding / drawing** | Words + visuals; concept maps and drawing have experimental support | Useful add-on |
| **Elaboration / self-explanation** | "Ask why/how"; moderate utility | Useful, pairs with retrieval |
| **Summarization** | Low-moderate utility | OK only if generative |
| **Highlighting / underlining** | **Low utility** | Mostly wasted time |
| **Rereading** | **Low utility** | Feels productive, isn't (the "illusion of fluency") |

Effect sizes are from the Q1 sources (Dunlosky et al. 2013; Donoghue & Hattie 2021; Carpenter, Pan &
Butler 2022). Quote the number, then the tactic.

## The two that beat everything

**1. Retrieval practice** — actively recall from memory instead of looking at notes. Forms: flashcards
(Anki), practice questions, past papers, or "blank page testing" (Cornell LSC) — shut the book, write
everything you remember, then check gaps. The act of struggling to recall is what builds durable
memory.

**2. Spaced practice** — the same total hours spread across days beats one long session. Forgetting is
the mechanism: each time you let memory fade a little and then retrieve, the memory strengthens
(Bjork's "desirable difficulties"). A simple review ladder: revisit at day 1, 3, 7, 14. Spaced-
repetition apps (Anki) automate the schedule.

**Combine them: spaced retrieval practice** is the single highest-leverage study habit — repeatedly
testing yourself at increasing intervals (research-synthesis.md Q1/Q4).

## Why students resist the good ones

Rereading and highlighting feel effective because familiarity is mistaken for mastery — the **illusion
of fluency**. The good techniques feel *harder* (that's the point) and produce better results. Naming
this for a student is often what unlocks the behavior change.

## How to apply it (the move for almost any "how do I study X" question)

1. Convert the material into questions (you, or a tool) as you first learn it.
2. Self-test from those questions instead of rereading.
3. Space the tests out over days; review what you got wrong sooner.
4. For problem subjects, interleave problem types rather than blocking one type.
5. Explain it out loud in plain words (Feynman) to expose gaps.
