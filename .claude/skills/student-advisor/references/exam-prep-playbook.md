# Exam Prep Playbook

How to prepare for an exam, grounded in `research-synthesis.md` Q3 (and Q1 for the underlying
techniques). The whole strategy in one line: **practice-test under exam conditions, on a spaced
schedule, and protect your sleep.** Cramming works for tomorrow and fails the semester.

## The evidence, briefly

- **Distributed beats massed** (2025 meta-analytic review of classroom research). Spreading the same
  hours across days dramatically outperforms cramming for retention and transfer.
- **Practice testing + feedback** improves performance and completion (MOOC RCT, N=6,100; physics
  exam study where practice exams + targeted homework beat a single practice exam).
- **Test anxiety** tracks with surface/passive study and worse outcomes; a concrete, realistic plan
  reduces it, and sleep protects memory consolidation.

(Some blogs cite "score 12-18% higher" — that specific figure isn't traceable to a peer-reviewed
source, so lead with the direction, not the number.)

## The plan, by time remaining

**If you have 2+ weeks:** this is the ideal case for spacing.
1. **Map the exam:** past papers + syllabus → rank topics by exam weight × your weakness. Spend ~70%
   of time on high-priority/weak topics.
2. **Pass 1 (breadth):** work through every topic once for understanding, turning notes into questions.
   Don't aim for mastery yet.
3. **Pass 2 (retrieval):** self-test from your questions and past papers; review what you miss sooner
   (spaced ladder: 1-3-7-14 days).
4. **Pass 3 (consolidation, final days):** full timed past papers under exam conditions; fix the last
   gaps. **Buffer ~30% of your schedule** for slippage and review.

**If you have ~1 week:** compress the same three passes; prioritize past-paper practice testing over
rereading; still space across the days you have.

**If it's tomorrow (cramming):** you can't get the spacing benefit, so maximize **active recall** on
the highest-weight topics, do one past paper, sleep (don't trade sleep for marginal review — sleep
consolidates what you studied), and accept this is damage control, not a strategy to repeat.

## A concrete weekly skeleton (2-week example)

```
Days 1-4  (breadth):     all topics once, build question banks, light self-test
Days 5-9  (retrieval):   daily self-quiz + past-paper sections on weak topics, spaced review
Days 10-12 (consolidate): full timed past papers, fix gaps
Day 13:    light review of flagged items only, early night
Day 14:    exam — quick warm-up recall, no new material
```

## Test anxiety

- A realistic written schedule removes the "I don't know if I'll be ready" stressor — much of exam
  anxiety is uncertainty.
- Practice under **real conditions** (timed, no notes) so the exam format isn't novel.
- Protect sleep, especially the night before; the forgetting curve drops fastest in the first 24h, so
  review new material soon after first exposure rather than pulling an all-nighter.
- Basic regulation (breathing, arriving early, not cramming in the corridor) helps; if anxiety is
  severe/clinical, point to campus counselling, not just study tips.
