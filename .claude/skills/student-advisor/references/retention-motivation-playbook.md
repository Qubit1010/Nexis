# Retention & Motivation Playbook

Two linked problems: making material *stick* (retention) and getting yourself to *start and continue*
(motivation), including in subjects you dislike. Grounded in `research-synthesis.md` Q4 (and Q1).

## Retention: beat the forgetting curve

The core fact: memory fades predictably, and the fix is **spaced retrieval** — revisiting material at
increasing intervals, recalling it from memory each time.

- "I get it in class, then it's gone a week later" is the **illusion of fluency** plus normal
  forgetting. Understanding once is not the same as being able to retrieve later.
- **The fix:** turn material into questions and self-test on a spacing ladder (day 1, 3, 7, 14...),
  reviewing missed items sooner. Spaced-repetition apps (Anki) automate the optimal timing; the
  algorithms behind them measurably improve long-term retention (PNAS 2019).
- **Make encoding richer:** explain it in plain words (Feynman), connect it to things you already
  know (elaboration), and use visuals for structure (dual coding). Richer encoding + spaced retrieval
  is what makes knowledge durable.

See `learning-science.md` for the technique evidence; this is the same machinery aimed at *durability*.

## Motivation: the one principle

**Motivation follows action, not the other way around.** You will rarely *feel* like studying a
subject you dislike before you start. So stop waiting for motivation and engineer the start.

- **The 2-minute rule:** commit to just two minutes. The hardest part of any session is the first two
  minutes; once engaged, momentum carries. Lower the bar to "open the book and do one problem."
- **Shrink the task:** large, vague tasks ("study macroeconomics") trigger avoidance. Define a small,
  concrete next action ("do 3 practice questions on elasticity").
- **Design the environment:** remove friction (materials out the night before, phone in another room),
  and add friction to the distractions.

## Creating interest in a subject you dislike (Self-Determination Theory)

SDT (Ryan & Deci) says intrinsic motivation grows when three needs are met — **autonomy, competence,
relatedness**. Practical translation for a boring required course:

- **Autonomy:** choose *how* you engage (your own examples, your own order, a project angle), and
  reconnect to *why* it matters to a goal you actually hold.
- **Competence:** start at a level you can win at. Boredom is often disguised overwhelm — close the
  gap with easier reps first, and competence breeds interest.
- **Relatedness:** study with someone, teach it to a friend, or tie it to a field you love (e.g., a CS
  student finding the algorithms inside the dull subject).
- **Manufacture curiosity:** pose a question you genuinely want answered before reading; let the
  material answer it. You don't need to love organic chemistry — you need to make studying it less
  painful and short enough that resistance doesn't kick in.

## Beating procrastination with habits, not willpower

Willpower is unreliable; habits are durable. Build the behavior so studying is the path of least
resistance:

- **Habit loop (cue → routine → reward):** attach a clear cue and a small reward to studying.
- **Habit stacking:** bolt studying onto an existing daily anchor ("after dinner, 25 minutes of
  recall").
- **Identity-based change:** aim to *become* "someone who studies daily," not "someone trying to
  study." Small consistent wins reinforce the identity.
- **Productive reasons (Princeton McGraw):** motivate from learning/achievement goals, not fear of
  failure or looking stupid — fear-based motivation is powerful but corrosive and feeds procrastination.
