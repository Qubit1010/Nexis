# What Not To Do — Debunked Study Myths & Traps

Correct these when a student repeats them. Each has a research-backed replacement (see
`research-synthesis.md` + `learning-science.md`). Lead with the better behavior, not just the "no."

## Study technique myths

- **"Rereading is studying."** Low utility (Dunlosky 2013). It builds the *illusion of fluency*, not
  durable memory. → Self-test from memory instead (retrieval practice).
- **"Highlighting/underlining helps."** Low utility. Passive marking ≠ learning. → Turn the highlighted
  bits into questions and quiz yourself.
- **"Cramming the night before works."** Massed practice loses to distributed practice for retention.
  → Space study across days; if you must cram, do active recall on high-weight topics and then sleep.
- **"Learning styles" (visual/auditory/kinesthetic).** No credible evidence that matching teaching to
  a "style" improves learning — it's one of the most persistent myths. → Match the method to the
  *material* (dual coding, worked examples), not to a supposed personal style.
- **"Pen always beats laptop."** Overstated. With distractions removed, the medium is roughly a wash
  (Voyer meta-analysis). → What matters is **generative** notes (your words) + **reviewing by self-
  testing**. Type fine, just don't transcribe verbatim.
- **"Marathon study sessions = dedication."** Long unbroken sessions fight spacing and focus. → Focused
  40-90 min blocks, spaced across days.
- **"I'll study when I feel motivated."** Motivation follows action. → Use the 2-minute rule and
  environment design to start; momentum creates the motivation.

## Exam traps

- Spending the first week on topics you already know and running out of time for weak ones. → Rank by
  exam weight × weakness; spend ~70% on high-priority/weak topics.
- Trading sleep for marginal last-minute review. → Sleep consolidates memory; protect it, especially
  the night before.
- Treating "I understand it" as "I can produce it under exam conditions." → Practice with **timed past
  papers**, no notes.

## Careers / grad-school / scholarship traps

- **Assuming a master's or PhD is required for AI/ML jobs.** For most industry roles it isn't —
  portfolio + skills + experience usually win (see `grad-school-playbook.md`). A PhD is for research
  roles; a master's for a deliberate trajectory change.
- **Chasing credentials over shipped projects.** The 2026 differentiator is real systems you've built
  end-to-end, not certificates or notebooks.
- **"Prompt engineering is my specialization."** It's now table-stakes, not a standalone edge. Go
  deeper (RAG, agents, eval, deployment).
- **Applying to scholarships last-minute / when ineligible.** The top rejection cause. Read eligibility
  twice; start months ahead; apply to 3-5 in parallel; write a *specific* return plan.
- **Picking a study-abroad country by ranking alone.** Choose by **post-study work visa + PR pathway +
  all-in cost**, not brochure prestige.
- **Paying for IELTS when an MOI certificate is accepted** (DAAD, CSC China, GKS Korea, others).

## Honesty traps for the skill itself

- Don't quote a precise stat (effect size, salary, deadline, "X% higher") that isn't in
  `research-synthesis.md` or the live notebook. Several study-blog figures (e.g., "12-18% higher") are
  **not** traceable to peer-reviewed sources — give the direction, not a fake number.
- Salary, job-market, scholarship, and visa figures **age fast** — verify via `notebook-live-query.md`
  before presenting as current, and flag anything net-new from a live query.
- For current named resources/courses or papers, hand to `deep-research` / `assignment-research` rather
  than inventing links.
