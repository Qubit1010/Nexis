# Voss Framework — 5-Phase Conversation Map

Loaded in Scenario B (reply drafter). This is how you read where a LinkedIn
conversation is actually sitting and pick the next move without jumping ahead.

The big idea from *Never Split the Difference*: the person you're talking to
wants to feel understood before they want to be sold to. You earn the right to
the next phase by doing the current one well. Skipping a phase feels pushy and
costs trust. Staying too long in one feels aimless.

---

## The 5 phases

Every LinkedIn conversation after a connection accept tends to move through
these five states. Your job in any given reply is to **match their current
phase and move them one step forward** — not two, not zero.

### 1. Qualify — "what's actually going on?"

**They look like this:** just accepted. Short reply like "Happy to connect"
or "What do you do?" or a generic pleasantry. No disclosed pain, no signal on
what they care about.

**Your move:** a calibrated question. Calibrated = open-ended, starts with
*how* or *what*, impossible to answer with a yes/no. The question should
surface *their* state — not pitch yours.

- "How are you handling [thing their role touches] at [company] right now?"
- "What's the part of the business that's eating the most of your week?"
- "What does a typical week look like at your stage right now?"

**Why this works:** people love talking about their own situation. A good
calibrated question costs them nothing to answer but gives you the one thing
you don't have yet: a real problem to label.

**Do not:** pitch, drop a case study, or ask for a call here. Nothing is
earned yet.

---

### 2. Label — "name the pain back to them"

**They look like this:** disclosed something. Even a small thing counts.
"Yeah we're a small team." "Onboarding is brutal." "We're figuring out our
CRM." That's a crack in the door.

**Your move:** a Voss label. Labels start with *It sounds like...*, *It
looks like...*, *It seems like...*, *It feels like...* — never "I think"
or "I believe" (that makes it about you). The label mirrors their reality
back to them in slightly sharper language than they used.

- "It sounds like the onboarding piece is the part that's quietly costing
  you the most hours."
- "It seems like the CRM isn't the real problem — the real problem is that
  nobody has time to keep it clean."
- "It looks like you're at the stage where the founder is the integration
  layer between the tools."

**Why this works:** a good label gets a *"yeah, exactly"* or *"that's right."*
That moment is when they stop evaluating you and start feeling understood.
A correct label earns the right to move forward. A wrong label gets corrected
by them — which is also useful, because now you know more.

**Do not:** follow the label with a pitch in the same message. Let it land.
End the message on the label or on a short follow-up calibrated question.

---

### 3. Proof — "drop one relevant result"

**They look like this:** confirmed the label. Asked a specific question
like "what do you actually do?" or "how would you fix that?" or volunteered
more detail about the problem.

**Your move:** one result from Aleem's work that maps to their specific
pain. One. Not a list. Not a portfolio dump.

Use the results bank in SKILL.md. Pick the single closest match.

- Their pain: manual onboarding → "Built a client onboarding pipeline for a
  SaaS team that cut manual work by 70%."
- Their pain: lead follow-up eating the week → "Built a lead-to-outreach
  pipeline that cut manual follow-up time by 80% and removed 3 human
  handoffs."
- Their pain: slow email/CRM handling → "Built an AI-powered email + CRM
  workflow that cut response time from hours to minutes."

**Why this works:** one specific, quantified, relevant result lands harder
than a paragraph of capabilities. The brain remembers numbers. The brain
remembers specificity. "70%" sticks. "We help with automation" doesn't.

**Do not:** ask for the call immediately after proof. Let them pull.

---

### 4. Pull — "let them ask for it"

**They look like this:** engaged with the result. Asked how long it took.
Asked what tools you used. Asked if you've done it for their industry.
Asked about cost-lite signals ("how would something like that work?").
Asked about your availability.

**Your move:** give them the specific thing they asked for, briefly. Then
drop a short hook that invites the next step without asking for it.

- "Took about 3 weeks start to finish. The heavy lift was mapping their
  existing tool stack — the actual automation layer went fast once we
  understood the handoffs."
- "Used n8n for the workflow layer, OpenAI for the enrichment, and a
  lightweight Postgres setup for state. Nothing exotic — the architecture
  matters more than the tools."

**Why this works:** pull means they're selling themselves on it. The
moment you stop pushing is usually the moment they lean in. Giving them
the specific answer respects the fact that they asked — but not expanding
into a pitch respects the fact that they haven't asked for one yet.

**Do not:** sneak a call ask into this reply. If they ask "would this work
for us?" — that's still pull, not a call signal. Describe how it would
work for them and let them surface the meeting themselves.

---

### 5. Call — "no-oriented ask, permission-framed"

**They look like this:** clear pull signal. Said something like "I'd want
to see this." "When could we talk?" "What does a first project look
like?" "Do you have time this week?" Or — after 2-3 rounds of engagement
— the conversation has plateaued and you need to either move or let it
cool.

**Your move:** a no-oriented, permission-based call ask. No-oriented
questions start with *Would it be off base...*, *Is it a bad idea...*,
*Would it be crazy...*, *Is this the worst timing...*. They give the
other person the feeling of control — saying no is easy, which paradoxically
makes saying yes easy too.

- "Would it be off base to grab 15 minutes this week so I can show you
  exactly what that onboarding automation looks like end-to-end?"
- "Is it a bad idea to hop on a short call so I can map this against your
  specific flow before scoping anything?"
- "Would it be crazy to do a 20-minute scope conversation this week and
  see if there's actually a fit?"

**Why this works:** "yes-oriented" asks ("Do you want to jump on a call?")
create pressure and trigger a polite-no reflex. "No-oriented" asks feel
low-stakes and honest. People who say *"no, it's not off base at all"* are
effectively saying yes — but they feel like they chose it.

**Do not:** apologize ("sorry to ask..."), hedge ("if you have time at
all..."), or propose 3 time options in the first call ask. Make it clean,
one sentence, one question.

## How to read where they are

Run this quick diagnostic on the latest message they sent you:

| What they said | What phase you're entering |
|----------------|----------------------------|
| "Happy to connect" / "What do you do?" / generic | **Qualify** — ask a calibrated question |
| Disclosed a pain, a constraint, or a frustration | **Label** — mirror it back |
| Agreed with your label OR asked "what do you do exactly?" | **Proof** — one result |
| Engaged with the result, asked specifics | **Pull** — describe, don't pitch |
| Asked about time / cost-lite / "how would we start" | **Call** — no-oriented ask |

If you're unsure between two phases, pick the *earlier* one. Slower is
almost always safer than faster on LinkedIn.

## Special cases

### Peer / agency owner / AI consultant replied

If the person you're talking to *does what Aleem does* — they run an
agency, they're an AI consultant, they're a builder themselves — they are
almost never a direct client. They're a **referral partner or collaborator.**

Pivot framing immediately:
- Don't pitch services
- Treat them as a peer
- Ask whether they work with specialists on the build side / overflow
  work / subcontract partnerships
- Calibrated question examples:
  - "Do you handle the technical builds yourself, or bring in specialists
    for that side?"
  - "How are you structured — in-house, partnered, or ad-hoc per project?"

This is still Qualify phase — but a peer-track version of it.

### They went cold mid-thread

If they replied a few times then stopped, don't re-open with "just
checking in." Instead, share something genuinely useful — a pattern
you're seeing, a short observation — and soft-ask if it resonates. This
is a soft re-entry to whichever phase they last engaged with.

### They disclosed pain but then went silent

Don't chase with a pitch. Send a label-only message. Labels pull responses
far more reliably than questions do, because the person feels compelled
to correct or confirm them.

## Rules that apply in every phase

- **Match their message length.** One-line reply in → one-line reply back.
  Four-paragraph reply in → you earned the right to a longer response, but
  still keep it tight.
- **Every outgoing message has at least one label.** Even in Proof and
  Pull. The label is what keeps the conversation feeling human.
- **Never mention price, rate, or "how much".** On LinkedIn, the call is
  where price conversations start. Not the DM.
- **No corporate vocabulary.** No "circle back," no "touch base," no
  "reach out," no "leverage," no "synergies," no "bandwidth."
- **No em dashes. No emojis.** Hyphens and commas only.
- **The meta-line (Phase + Tactic) is for Aleem, not the prospect.** Never
  include it in what gets pasted into LinkedIn.

## Worked micro-examples

**Them:** "Happy to connect, Aleem."
**You (Qualify):** "Appreciate the connect, [Name]. It looks like [Company]
is at a stage where the ops side starts quietly eating the week — how are
you handling that right now?"

**Them:** "Yeah onboarding is a mess honestly. We're hiring faster than we
can document."
**You (Label):** "It sounds like the real pain isn't the hiring pace — it's
that the documentation layer hasn't caught up, so every new person
re-learns what the last one figured out. That's the expensive version of
the problem."

**Them:** "Exactly. How would you even fix that?"
**You (Proof):** "Built a client onboarding pipeline for a SaaS team
recently that cut their manual onboarding work by 70% — the core move was
turning the tribal knowledge into a structured flow the system runs,
instead of leaving it in people's heads."

**Them:** "Interesting. How long did something like that take?"
**You (Pull):** "About 3 weeks. Most of the time was mapping what they
actually did versus what they thought they did — the build itself went
fast once the flow was clear."

**Them:** "That's exactly what we need. When could we talk?"
**You (Call):** "Would it be off base to grab 20 minutes this week so I
can map it against your specific onboarding flow before scoping anything?"

Five messages. Clean progression. No pitch dump, no pressure, no
corporate tone.
