# Never Split the Difference -- Negotiation Framework

## Core Philosophy

- **Negotiation is emotional, not rational.** People decide with emotion (System 1) and justify with logic (System 2). Soothe the animal mind first before presenting arguments.
- **Tactical Empathy** is not being nice. It is understanding their perspective so completely they feel safe enough to tell you the truth. Intellectual awareness of their emotional state, not an emotional reaction.
- **"That's Right" vs "You're Right."** "You're right" means they are placating you to end the conversation. "That's right" means they feel genuinely understood. Always aim for "That's right."
- **Never split the difference.** Compromise is a lazy outcome that leaves both sides unhappy. A bad deal is worse than no deal.

## Techniques

### Mirroring

- **What:** Repeat the last 1-3 critical words they said, with upward inflection (like a question).
- **Why:** Forces them to elaborate and reveals hidden information without you asking. They feel heard.
- **Process:** Use Late-Night FM DJ voice, start with "I'm sorry...", mirror, then silence (4+ seconds).
- **Marketing use cases:**
  - Discovery calls: Mirror their pain point to get them to elaborate ("Losing 15 hours a week?")
  - Objection handling: Mirror the objection back ("The budget is capped?") to get the real constraint
  - Scope creep conversations: Mirror the request to force them to justify it

### Labeling

- **Phrases:** "It seems like...", "It sounds like...", "It looks like..."
- **Never use:** "I hear you..." or "I think..." -- "I" puts focus on you, not them.
- **Rule:** Label, then shut up. Let it sink in. Do not explain or fill the silence.
- **Negative labels** diffuse anger: "It seems like you're frustrated with how long this is taking."
- **Positive labels** reinforce behavior: "It seems like you value long-term partnerships."
- **Application to cold emails and DMs:**
  - Subject lines: "It looks like [COMPANY] is scaling fast"
  - Email openers: "It seems like your team is spending a lot of time on [MANUAL_PROCESS]"
  - Follow-ups: "It sounds like timing wasn't right last week"

### Accusation Audit

- **What:** List every terrible thing they could think about you before they say it. Steals their thunder.
- **Phrase:** "I have a confession. You're going to think I'm [worst thing]. You're going to think I'm [second worst thing]."
- **Effect:** They instinctively defend you: "No, no, you're not that bad!"
- **Application to cold outreach:**
  - "I know you probably get dozens of emails like this, and I'm guessing your first instinct is to delete it."
  - "You're probably thinking this is just another agency pitch..."
  - Pre-empt pricing objections: "You're going to think this number is high."

### Calibrated Questions

- **Rules:** Start with "How" or "What." Never "Why" (sounds like an accusation). Avoid "Is/Do/Does/Can" (leads to Yes/No).
- **Key questions:**
  - "How am I supposed to do that?" -- the ultimate weapon. Says "No" without saying no. Forces them to empathize and solve your problem.
  - "What about this is important to you?" -- digs for hidden motivations.
  - "How does this affect the rest of your team?" -- uncovers hidden stakeholders.
  - "What is the biggest challenge you face with [AREA]?" -- discovery opener.
  - "How would you like me to proceed?" -- diffuses anger.
  - "How can we solve this problem?" -- neutralizes threats.
- **Application to discovery and objection handling:**
  - Client lowballs: "How am I supposed to deliver high-quality automation for that price?"
  - Scope creep: "How does adding this feature affect the original deadline we agreed on?"
  - Stalled deals: "What's the biggest concern holding this back?"

### The Late-Night FM DJ Voice

- Deep, slow, calm, downward inflection. Use 80% of the time. Signals control without threat.
- **In written communication:** Short sentences. Calm tone. No exclamation marks. Measured pacing. Confidence without aggression. Use periods, not question marks, when making statements.
- **Playful/Smiling Voice** (20%): Light and encouraging. Use to de-escalate tension.
- **Assertive Voice:** Avoid entirely. Triggers resistance.

### No-Oriented Questions

- People feel safe saying "No." Rephrase questions to invite "No" instead of fishing for "Yes."
- "Is now a bad time to talk?" beats "Do you have a few minutes?"
- "Is this a ridiculous idea?" beats "Is this a good idea?"
- "Have you given up on this project?" -- the magic email for ghosting clients. Triggers loss aversion.
- "Are you against moving the deadline to ensure quality?"

## The Ackerman Bargaining Model

1. Set your Target Price (e.g., $1,000)
2. First offer: 65% of target ($650). Expect rejection.
3. Second offer: 85% ($850). Big jump shows you are stretching.
4. Third offer: 95% ($950). Smaller jump signals you are running out of room.
5. Final offer: 100% ($1,000).
6. Use a precise, non-round number ($1,037 not $1,000). Precise numbers look calculated, not guessed.
7. Throw in a non-monetary item: "I can't go lower than $1,037, but I can include a free 30-minute training session."

**Application to pricing discussions:**
- If they cannot pay full rate, pivot to non-monetary terms:
  - Video testimonial on LinkedIn within 3 days of completion
  - 100% payment upfront instead of Net-30
  - Email introduction to a specific person/company
  - Portfolio rights to use the work as a case study

## Process: Discovery to Shaping Reality to Bargaining

### Phase 1: Discovery and Safety
1. Start with "No": "Is now a bad time to talk?" (gives them control)
2. Mirror and Label: Keep them talking until you find their emotional driver
3. Summary: Paraphrase their situation + Label their emotions
4. Goal: Get them to say "That's Right"

### Phase 2: Shaping Reality
1. Anchor high: Start with an extreme anchor to reset expectations
2. Use ranges: "Top consultants in this field get between $5K and $8K" (anchors to $5K)
3. Loss aversion framing: "Every day you don't automate this, you're losing $X in wasted hours"
4. Neutralize "Fair": If they say "I just want what's fair," respond with "It seems like you're ready to provide evidence that I've been unfair"

### Phase 3: Bargaining
1. Ackerman Model (65/85/95/100 + precise number)
2. Rule of Three: Get them to agree 3 times in one conversation to prevent flaking
3. Watch for "I'll try" -- it means "I plan to fail." Revert to "How" questions.
4. Spot liars: 7-38-55 Rule (7% words, 38% tone, 55% body language). If mismatch, trust tone/body.

## Application to Marketing

### Cold Emails
- Labels in subject lines: "It looks like [COMPANY] is growing fast"
- Empathy in openers: "It seems like your team spends a lot of time on [PAIN_POINT]"
- Accusation audit in first line: "I know you probably get tons of emails like this"
- No-oriented CTA: "Would it be a bad idea if I sent over a quick link?"

### LinkedIn DMs
- Mirror their profile/posts: Reference something specific they said
- Label their situation: "It seems like you're scaling [THING] right now"
- Micro-commitment CTA, not "book a call"

### Follow-ups
- Ghosting email: "Have you given up on this project?" (triggers loss aversion, almost always gets a reply)
- Label the silence: "It sounds like the timing wasn't right"
- No-oriented: "Have I completely missed the mark here?"

### Pricing and Closing Conversations
- Never give a round number. $4,750 beats $5,000.
- Use ranges to anchor: "Projects like this typically run between $3K and $6K"
- Frame as loss: "Every week without this automation costs your team roughly [X] hours"
- Pivot to non-monetary if price is stuck: testimonials, referrals, faster payment, portfolio rights
- Rule of Three to lock commitment before ending the call
