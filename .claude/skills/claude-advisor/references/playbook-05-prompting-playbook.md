# Beginner - Section 5: The Beginner's Prompting Playbook

*Six moves that make every answer dramatically better.*

**Bottom line:** Great results do not come from clever wording. They come from briefing Claude the way you would brief a sharp new hire: give it context, tell it exactly what you want, show it an example, and let it ask questions. Master these six moves and you will outperform most people using the same tool.

---

The biggest mistake beginners make is treating Claude like a vending machine: type a short request, expect a perfect answer. Treat it instead like a brilliant assistant on their first day. It is capable, but it knows nothing about you, your business, or what good looks like until you tell it.

## 1. Give it context (the biggest lever)

Before the request, tell Claude what it needs to know: who you are, who the output is for, and any relevant background. The same question with context attached produces a completely different answer. If the context lives in a document, upload it. If it is ongoing, put it in a Project so you never repeat yourself.

## 2. Ask it to ask you questions first

One line changes everything: "Before you answer, ask me any questions you need to do this well." Instead of guessing and giving you something generic, Claude surfaces the gaps in your request and fills them in. This single habit prevents most bad first drafts.

## 3. Set the role and the format

Tell Claude who to be ("act as my marketing assistant," "you are a careful financial analyst") and exactly how you want the output (length, structure, tone). "A 120-word friendly email with one call to action" beats "write an email" every time. Vague in, vague out.

## 4. Show an example

If you have a sample of what good looks like, a past email you liked, a report in the right format, paste it in and say "match this style." Examples teach Claude your taste faster than any description. This is how you stop output from sounding generic.

## 5. Iterate, do not restart

The first answer is a draft, not a verdict. Stay in the same conversation and steer: "shorter," "more direct," "drop the second paragraph," "make the tone warmer." Claude keeps the context and refines. Starting a brand-new chat throws away everything it just learned.

## 6. Make it check its work

For anything that matters, add "review your answer for mistakes before finalizing" or "double-check these numbers." Claude is comparatively careful, but it can still be confidently wrong. Asking it to verify catches a surprising number of errors, and you still confirm the important facts yourself.

## Weak prompt vs strong prompt

| Weak | Strong |
|------|--------|
| "Write a marketing email." | "You are my marketing assistant for a boutique accounting firm. Write a friendly cold email under 120 words to small business owners offering a free bookkeeping review, with one clear call to action. Match the voice of this example: [paste]. Before you write, ask me anything you need." |

## The reusable skeleton: R-C-T-F-E

**ROLE** (who Claude should be) + **CONTEXT** (the background and files) + **TASK** (exactly what you want) + **FORMAT** (length, structure, tone) + **EXAMPLE** (a sample of good output). Then close with: "ask me questions before you start."

Keep this five-part pattern in mind and your prompts write themselves.

## Key takeaways

- Brief Claude like a new hire, not a vending machine. Context is the single biggest lever.
- Add "ask me questions first" to almost every real request; it kills generic drafts.
- Set the role and format, and show an example to teach Claude your taste.
- Iterate in the same conversation instead of restarting, and make it check its work on anything important.
- Remember R-C-T-F-E: Role, Context, Task, Format, Example.
