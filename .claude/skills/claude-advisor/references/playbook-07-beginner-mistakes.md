# Beginner - Section 7: The 7 Beginner Mistakes (and How to Fix Them)

*Most underuse of Claude traces back to a small number of the same errors.*

**Bottom line:** The difference between people who say "Claude is okay" and people who say "Claude changed how I work" almost always comes down to these seven habits. None of them require technical skill. They just require a shift in approach.

---

## Mistake 1: Blank-chat syndrome

**What it looks like:** Opening a new chat for every question, with no context, and wondering why the answers feel generic.

**The fix:** Build Projects (Section 3). Load your context once, set custom instructions once, and every conversation inside that Project starts already knowing your world.

## Mistake 2: Vague asks

**What it looks like:** "Write me a proposal." "Summarize this." "Make it better."

**The fix:** Apply the R-C-T-F-E skeleton from Section 5. Role, Context, Task, Format, Example. If you cannot tell a new hire exactly what you want in one paragraph, Claude cannot figure it out either.

## Mistake 3: Accepting the first answer

**What it looks like:** Getting a mediocre output, shrugging, and moving on. Or concluding that Claude is not useful.

**The fix:** The first answer is a draft. Stay in the conversation and steer: "shorter," "more direct," "cut the second paragraph," "make the opening line stronger." Claude improves fast when you iterate, and iteration takes seconds.

## Mistake 4: No examples

**What it looks like:** Describing the tone you want in vague words like "professional but warm" and getting output that sounds nothing like you.

**The fix:** Paste an example. A past email, a piece of writing you liked, a paragraph in the right voice. Showing beats telling every time.

## Mistake 5: The run-on prompt

**What it looks like:** One massive request that asks Claude to do seven things at once, with no structure.

**The fix:** Break complex tasks into steps. Do them in the same conversation so Claude keeps the context, but ask for one thing at a time. "First, give me an outline. Good, now write section 1. Good, now make it shorter." Sequential is better than simultaneous.

## Mistake 6: Trusting blindly

**What it looks like:** Copying Claude's output directly into a proposal, email, or report without verifying the facts or numbers.

**The fix:** Verify anything that matters: names, numbers, quotes, sources, legal claims. Add "check your work before finalizing" to high-stakes prompts. Claude is careful, but it can still be confidently wrong. You are the editor.

## Mistake 7: Never using Projects

**What it looks like:** Re-uploading the same documents, re-explaining your brand voice, re-stating your role, in every conversation.

**The fix:** One Project per role or per client. Set it up once (Section 3). This is the single biggest quality-of-life upgrade for anyone who uses Claude more than a few times a week.

---

## 30-second self-check

Before you send any prompt, ask yourself:

- Did I give it context, or am I starting blank?
- Is the task specific enough that a smart new hire would know what to do?
- Did I include an example of what good looks like?
- Is this going in a Project where the context already lives?

If you answer yes to all four, the output will almost always be usable. If any answer is no, take 30 seconds to fix it before hitting send.

## Key takeaways

- The biggest mistakes are not technical. They are about context, specificity, and iteration.
- Vague prompts get vague answers. The R-C-T-F-E skeleton fixes this.
- Iterate inside the same conversation rather than starting over with a new chat.
- Verify anything important. Claude is capable but not infallible.
- Projects eliminate 80% of the "I keep re-explaining myself" frustration.
