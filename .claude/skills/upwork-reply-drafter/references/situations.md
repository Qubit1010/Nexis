# The Four Situations — per-situation reply playbook

Each Upwork reply lands in one of four situations. Diagnose which, set the goal, run the research-backed moves, and reach for the framework that fits. All claims trace to `research-synthesis.md` (Q1-Q8). The frameworks referenced (Voss, Hormozi value equation, objection-psychology) live in `.claude/skills/sales-playbook/frameworks/` and are bundled into the dashboard prompt.

**One rule across all four:** never the same shape twice. Vary the opener, the length, the route to the point. A reply that reads like a template loses (Q8).

---

## 1. Pre-hire Q&A + negotiation

**You are:** answering a client who replied to a proposal, or is pushing on price/scope/timeline before hiring.
**Goal:** win the contract without discounting — move toward an explicit contract offer.
**DB stage:** `pre_hire` (answering questions) → `negotiating` (price/scope in play).

**Moves (Q1, Q2):**
- Structure every reply: **restate the outcome in one line → answer their actual question → one low-friction next step** (a scoped first milestone, a call, or a scope confirmation). Stop selling once they've replied; start starting (Q1).
- Ask **one** clarifying question, never five (Q1).
- On "can you do it cheaper?" → **move scope, never the rate** (Q2). First ask their real budget, then reduce scope to fit ("which parts would you prioritize?" — let them cut), or add value at the same price (faster date, extra revision, 30-day support), or walk. Never defend hours; defend the outcome.
- Anchor rate to scope, quote it as a conclusion: name the hours/scope first, then the number (Q2).
- **Close explicitly** when aligned: "I'll set up a [fixed/hourly] contract for [deliverables] at [rate]. Once you accept, I start on [first deliverable]." Don't wait for the client to initiate (Q1).

**Framework fit:**
- **Objection-psychology** — a price objection is usually a value-visibility problem, not a number problem. Diagnose the distortion (circumstances=budget, self=risk/fit) before answering.
- **Hormozi value equation** — reframe cost as the outcome and the risk of getting it wrong (Chris Do's axis-shift, Q2).
- **Voss** — a calibrated "how" / "what" question ("what would need to be true for this to fit your budget?") surfaces the real constraint.

**Avoid:** discounting to win (resets every renewal, Q2); "just checking in"; re-pitching; credential lists; more than one follow-up (Q1).

## 2. Active project

**You are:** mid-project — replying to a status ping, client feedback, a blocker, or a scope-change / scope-creep request.
**Goal:** keep momentum, contain scope professionally, protect the JSS.
**DB stage:** `active`.

**Moves (Q3, Q4, Q7):**
- **Scope-change request → change control, not reaction:** acknowledge → re-anchor to the agreed milestone/scope → offer **Swap / Extend / Explore** (Q3). Never start out-of-scope work until a new milestone is funded in escrow. Route formal changes through Upwork's "Request Changes to an Offer."
- **Status / trust cadence:** confirm scope in writing at kickoff with exclusions + dates; short weekly updates (2-6 wk) or monthly (retainer); surface problems immediately — an early honest warning beats a surprise (Q7).
- **Feedback / criticism:** respond operationally, not defensively; document the resolution in the message thread (the paper trail protects the JSS, Q4).
- Keep everything on-platform (payment protection + record, Q7).

**Framework fit:**
- **Voss labeling + mirroring** — "It sounds like the priority shifted to X" / "sounds like timeline is the real pressure" defuses a tense feedback or scope moment before you propose the path.
- **Voss calibrated questions** — "how would you like me to prioritize this against [planned task]?" hands the client the Swap decision.
- **Selling posture** — calm, back-foot, one angle; you're a peer solving a problem, not defending turf.

**Avoid:** silently absorbing out-of-scope work (breeds resentment + a worse review, Q2/Q3); going quiet on a blocker; closing the contract yourself later (JSS negative, Q4).

## 3. Closeout + review ask

**You are:** delivering final work, wrapping up, and steering toward a 5-star review + a repeat/retainer opening.
**Goal:** land the review and open the door to more work.
**DB stage:** `delivered` (delivered, review pending) → `reviewed` (review in).

**Moves (Q5, Q4, Q6):**
- **Deliver with a bit of extra value** — a short explanation of what you did + helpful next steps. Exceeding expectations, early delivery, and proactive comms are what drive positive public AND private feedback (Q5).
- **Ask the CLIENT to end the contract** (it triggers Upwork's feedback prompt) — never close it yourself (Q4/Q5). Wording, appreciation-based, at delivery: *"If you have a moment, I'd appreciate an honest review of your experience. It helps me a lot as I build my Upwork profile. I'll leave feedback on my end as well."*
- Don't ask for a specific star rating or pressure; one gentle follow-up max if they're slow (Q5).
- **Plant the retainer / next step** near the close: name a recurring need (maintenance, monitoring, next phase) as a natural next step, not a pitch; offer to continue via "Propose New Contract" to keep fee-tier progress (Q6).

**Framework fit:**
- **Hormozi value equation** — frame the retainer as ongoing risk-reduction ("keep it protected and improving"), not "more hours."
- **Voss "that's right"** — get the client to articulate the win in their own words before the review ask; it primes a specific, strong review.

**Avoid:** begging for stars; nagging; closing the contract yourself; a generic "let me know if you need anything" with no concrete next step.

## 4. Reactivation

**You are:** re-opening a dormant past client (project completed, ideally a positive review).
**Goal:** reopen with a genuine reason and a low-friction next step — no hard pitch.
**DB stage:** `reactivation`.

**Moves (Q6):**
- Avoid generic "checking in." Send a brief, value-driven note that **references a specific past outcome** and offers **one specific low-friction next step** — a micro-audit, or a "protect and improve" maintenance sprint.
- **Time the touch to a natural cycle** — quarter end, a new feature launch, a season — to lift response.
- Give a clear **binary choice** to minimize decision effort.
- For NexusPoint specifically: the genuine reason is real — agentic AI crossed from "drafts" to "does the work" this year; lead with that shift, offer a free Ops Teardown, no pressure (see the reactivation message pattern already in use).

**Framework fit:**
- **Opener discipline (anti-cadence)** — one true, specific detail about their business or the past project; that's the one thing a template can't fake (Q8).
- **Voss no-oriented question** — "Would it be a bad idea to send you the one thing I'd automate first?" lowers the reply barrier.

**Avoid:** "just following up," "circling back," a hard sell, or a long message. Short, warm, one reason, one next step.
