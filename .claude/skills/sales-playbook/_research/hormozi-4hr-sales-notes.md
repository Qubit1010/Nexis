## A. Selling philosophy — the 21 beliefs (00:00–09:00)

Hormozi's framing: **sell with logic.** Emotional buyers get excited, buy, then refund ("buyer's remorse call two days later"). A rational foundation makes the decision *stick* when emotion fades — like a relationship. Answer BOTH sides: emotion lowers the action threshold (gets the step), logic makes it stick (makes a good customer). The further up-market you go, the more buyers decide logic-first. [hormozi-4hr @00:00–01:26]

The beliefs (paraphrased, de-brashed):

1. **People want to believe you.** They want to buy — your job is to help their logical brain justify the decision they already want to make. [@01:26]
2. **Selling happens before the ask; closing happens after.** You're "selling" the whole time; you only start "closing" the moment you solicit the sale. [@02:00]
3. **Obstacles vs objections.** An **obstacle** is disagreement *before* you ask for the sale; an **objection** is disagreement *after*. Easier to dissolve obstacles pre-ask than to fight objections post-ask. [@02:30, @16:00–17:46]
4. **Expect and plan for "no." It's not failure, it's expected.** If they'd have said yes on their own, you weren't necessary. **No is the job** — if they could decide alone, they'd already have sent the money. Sales is the first step of coaching. [@02:30–03:39]
5. **If you never get a gasp at the price, you went too low.** Anchor high, then a real price feels like a rounding error ($100k first makes $1k feel small). Train the team to be comfortable with the no and the gasp. [@03:09–04:08]
6. **Selling properly is the first step to becoming a coach.** How you sell (using logic) sets up the client's success later. [@04:08]
7. **Selling is helping prospects make decisions to help themselves** — you help them help themselves. [@04:34]
8. **Keep the prospect, not the sale, as the priority.** Vanish in the sale, magnify them. They care about themselves, not you. [@04:34–05:05]
9. **Seek to understand, not to argue — childlike curiosity.** On an objection: "Huh, that's interesting — tell me more about that." Exposure makes high-stakes closes feel normal (like training fighters not to over-breathe). You have this conversation thousands of times; they have it once. [@05:05–05:36]
10. **Closing is a dance, not a fight — seduction not force.** Don't beat them into submission. **Sell from your back foot:** "I'm good, I want to help you; if that means not doing this, I'm still with you." [@05:36–06:09]
11. **Selling is a transference of belief over a bridge of trust.** Requires (a) you genuinely believe (so you can transfer it) and (b) trust (so the transfer can cross). Cold streaks come from losing belief in *why* you sell, not losing skill. Filling your own conviction is one of the fastest ways to raise close rate. [@06:09–07:33]
12. **You can only build trust if you genuinely want to help.** Humans sniff out intention as a survival mechanism. People who can't close often only care about closing. [@07:07–07:33]
13. **Belief and trust are continua, not binaries** — not "do they trust you?" but *how much*. Deep conviction makes prospects question their own excuses. [@07:33–07:58]
14. **Closers ask hard questions because they genuinely care.** Only a hard question pierces and creates transformation. [@07:58–08:26]
15. **The person who cares most about the prospect wins the sale** — including caring more than the prospect cares about themselves. If you're more convicted, they'll question their own excuses. [@08:26–08:59]
16. **Record your sales calls.** Study your hot streaks (jokes, when you asked, how you overcame). Fastest way back into flow and to train new reps. [@08:59]
17. **Power = the ability to direct or influence others.** Sales is power. [@09:00, @00:08]

**Sales ethics (the release valve):** helping someone decide to help themselves does NOT mean "buy from you." Keep their goal at the center; be genuinely happy if they own their power and *don't* buy. When you can do that, sales pressure evaporates — the only stake is "did I help them?" You win every conversation because you changed the metric. And you sell *more*, because you're not attached. [@14:23–16:00]

## B. The three beliefs a prospect must hold before "yes" (18:08–19:36)

Before anyone buys, they must want the goal AND believe three things:

1. **The product will get them the goal — the way they want to get there.** (Hormozi learned the second half the hard way at Gym Launch: people wanted to lose weight but *their* way.) [@18:08–18:38]
2. **You and others will support them** — "is this true, and will the people around me back this decision?" [@18:38–19:06]
3. **It will work for *them* specifically**, not just everyone else ("I'm a snowflake — I have X condition / no audience / etc."). Counter with **pride-proof**: stack proof until it's more unreasonable *not* to believe than to believe. "How many people would you need to see for it to be unreasonable not to believe — 10? 50? Here's a thousand, tell me when to stop." A **logical** close, not emotional. [@19:06–20:03]

## C. The three distortions (Albert Ellis / CBT) — the root of every objection (20:03–22:51)

Hormozi traces every objection to Albert Ellis's three core cognitive distortions (the irrational demands people use to upset themselves). Objections are where the prospect **casts their power** to something outside their control.

1. **Circumstances** — "I must get what I want when I want it; if I don't, I can't stand it." Blames external conditions (time, money). [@22:20]
2. **Others** — "Other people must treat me fairly/kindly; if not, they're bad." (Authority: "I have to ask my partner/spouse/boss.") [@22:20–22:51]
3. **Self** — "I must do well or else I'm no good." (Avoidance: dodging the decision entirely.) [@22:51]

These are distortions — **not real**. If you can *understand* (not memorize) which distortion a prospect is in, you can hold the high-stakes conversation and walk them out of it. [@22:51–23:19]

## D. The onion of blame — how close you are to the sale (23:19–24:11)

Blame peels from outside → core. The layer the objection sits in tells you how far you are from yes:

- **Outer — Circumstances** (time, money, "other stuff"). Furthest from the sale.
- **Middle — Others** (spouse, kids, employees, partner). Closer.
- **Core — Self** ("it's me / my fault / I'm afraid"). Closest to the decision.

You may have to peel multiple layers. When someone says "I just have to think about it," you're often already talking to the decision-maker in power — you just need the yes. When they cast power away ("my circumstances / my partner"), you're further out. **Never get stuck at the top layer.** [@23:19–24:39]

## E. The five manifestations (24:11–25:34)

The three distortions surface as five practical excuses:

| # | Manifestation | Distortion | Sounds like |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Time** | Circumstances | "Not a good time," "I'm really busy," "maybe in the future" |
| 2 | **Money / price / value** | Circumstances | "Can't afford it," "too expensive" |
| 3 | **Fit** | Circumstances→Self | "Not sure it's for me," "I'm a special case," "I hate [required step]" |
| 4 | **Authority** | Others | "I have to talk to my partner/spouse/boss" |
| 5 | **Avoidance / stall** | Self | "I need to think about it," "let life decide" |

Move surface → core. Ideally you never get *stuck* anywhere if the prospect wants the outcome and you truly believe you can help. [@24:11–25:34]

## F. Overcomes — TIME (26:04–31:57)

Three angles, all re-voiced natural for text (never disagree; step into their shoes first):

- **Macro ("busy season, lots going on"):** "Do you want this to last long-term? And you'll probably be busy again in the future, right? Then isn't now — while you're busy — the best time to learn it? If you can only do it in perfect conditions, it falls apart the moment life gets busy again. And isn't 'busy' exactly when you'd want the most support?" [@26:56–28:13]
- **Micro ("I can't find the time in my day"):** don't say "check your screen-time." Tell it on yourself: "I used to say the same thing — then my wife pulled up my phone and went 'guess I found your time.'" Then: a good program's first move is cutting the 90% of low-value work that's making you feel overwhelmed. It's not about *adding* to your plate — it's removing what isn't working. Jeff Bezos has the same 24 hours. [@28:13–29:22]
- **When-then fallacy ("when I have time/money, I'll start"):** empathize, then name it: "That's the *when-then* fallacy — 'when I have X, I'll do Y' — but it flips the sequence. 'I'll pay for the thing that makes me money once I have money.' 'I'll get in shape once I have a six-pack.' The program is what *gets* you the time/money." A distortion, not real. [@29:22–31:57]

## G. Overcomes — MONEY (31:57–40:19)

Four lines of reasoning:

1. **"A lot for you is good."** "Is this a lot of money to you?" If yes: "That's exactly why you'll succeed — you'll actually try. Others buy this and coast. Do you want to draw a line in the sand and go all in? It's not about whether *this* works — I've proven that — it's whether *you* work. Put more on the line, higher odds you make it." [@31:57–33:13]
2. **Relative vs absolute.** "It's a lot in absolute terms, but relative to what it returns — if it adds $10k/month, or gets you [the outcome] — it's tiny. So it's not really about price, it's about whether you believe it'll do that. Which means the real question is belief, not money." (Sidesteps price into belief.) Optionally anchor vs an alternative (e.g. a $100k+ 4-yr degree that doesn't produce the outcome). [@33:13–35:06]
3. **"What's money for, anyway? Pay in time or money."** "You'll spend this money over the next 12 months either way. And you're going to buy this program regardless — the only question is whether you pay in money or in time. You can learn every lesson yourself — do you want it to take 12 weeks or 12 years? Buying knowledge is the only way to buy time; you buy other people's mistakes so you don't repeat them. 12 months from now, will your money have gone to stuff that got you nowhere, or to this?" [@35:06–36:38]
4. **Resourceful, not resources.** "The only difference between you-now and a self-made millionaire when they started is that they were *resourceful*, not that they had *resources* — they started broke too. (Nike / *Shoe Dog*: Knight got vendors to make payroll rather than fold.)" Then the mirror: "Ever had a surprise bill — taxes — come up, and somehow found the money? You're resourceful when it's for someone else or an emergency, just not for yourself. Choose to be resourceful for *you*." [@36:38–40:19]

## H. Overcomes — FIT (40:19–~44:00)

"Not sure it's a fit for me" (identity/vehicle mismatch). Angle captured:

- **New identity → new priorities.** "We vote with our dollars for what we care about. Show me where someone spends time + money and I'll show you their future. [Sephora story: two girls told 'now that you're becoming women, you budget for this every month' — new identity, new priorities.] You've had priorities aligned to your *old* identity — the you that spends on X. There's the you you want to be. We can draw the line now: keep walking the old road, or step into the new identity, and new priorities come with it. People who want to be rich invest in themselves and their skills." [@40:19–44:00]

## H2. Overcomes — FIT (continued) — the change-cost reframe (~54:00)

- **Pain-of-staying > pain-of-change.** "Fit really comes down to: is the pain you're in now less than the pain of the change it takes to get the next thing? If you step into the new identity, you're good — because it was never about *resources*, it's about *resourcefulness*." [hormozi-4hr @53:36–54:24]

## J. Overcomes — AUTHORITY ("I have to talk to my partner/spouse/boss") (~48:38–53:36)

Distortion: **Others**. One core idea: **support, not permission.** Process:

1. **Isolate + sidestep** (esp. when the other party isn't present): "What do you think they wouldn't like?" → they name it → you handle *that* real concern, which has nothing to do with the partner. [@48:38–49:26]
2. **The support-not-permission reframe:** "Do they approve of your current struggle? No? Then why would they be against the thing that fixes what they already don't like? And if the roles were reversed — if *they* needed this to hit their goal — you'd support them, right? So why not you?" [@49:26–50:27]
3. **The resentment line (the kicker):** "This is your life, not theirs. If you hand them the decision and two years from now nothing's changed, who do you blame? That's how resentment builds. Explain the decision to them — 'I'm doing this so I don't end up resenting you, and instead of complaining I'm going all in' — but ask for their *support*, not their *permission*." [@50:27–51:49]
4. **The guarantee nudge:** a short no-sweat/trial guarantee lets them "step in" without needing the partner's sign-off first. [@52:17–53:36]

Aleem-adaptation note: keep the *logic* (support-not-permission, isolate the real concern, roles-reversed), drop Hormozi's long comedic bits. In B2B this maps to "loop in your partner/CTO" — isolate whether they're personally sold first (this is the Cole Gordon double-tie-down already in `objection-riffs.md #3`), then make it a support conversation with a risk-reversal to de-risk the internal sell.

## K. Overcomes — AVOIDANCE / STALL ("I need to think about it") (~54:00–1:09:00)

Distortion: **Self** — the core layer. By the time they're here you've cleared the outside reasons and you're finally talking to the decision-maker in power. Hormozi splits it **past / present / future**. It's about *confronting the decision* (kindly), because "it doesn't take *time* to make a decision, it takes *information* — and you're the source of the information." [@1:00:55–1:01:21]

**PAST frames** (they're anchored in something behind them):
- **"This isn't a fast decision."** "You've been making this decision for years — the wanting is old. Deciding now just means finally acting on it." Add **sunk cost**: "You already invested [time/effort/the discovery call] to get here, because it matters to you — don't let the distortion stop you now." [@55:38–56:29]
- **Two fastest cut-through questions:** "What are you most afraid of happening if you buy? What's the worst thing you picture?" Then fill the silence lightly and get to the real fear. [@56:29–57:22]
- **"Don't let it burn you twice."** For "I tried something like this before": "You got burned once by the bad decision — don't get burned a second time by letting it stop you from a good one. (A bad 8th-grade relationship doesn't mean relationships aren't for you — there are good ones and bad ones.) You'll have to do the work either way; this just gets you there faster." [@57:22–58:39]
- **"You've waffled before — that's why you're here."** "Have you been in this spot and said no before? Do you think that's part of why you're still here? Want to change that? One decision changes it." [@58:39–59:30]
- **"Tired of another year of *almost*?"** "Almost hitting the goal, almost the revenue, almost the life — if you keep doing what you're doing, next year is another *almost*. The question isn't what it costs, it's what it's *already* cost you not to decide." (**cost of inaction**; we regret inactions more than actions.) [@59:30–59:52]

**PRESENT frame** (they just don't know how to decide):
- **The rocking-chair walk-through:** "You're not going home to sit and ponder this — you'll get in the car, pick up the kids, do the laundry, and in a few days nothing will have changed. So let's just decide now. It doesn't take *time* to decide, it takes *information*, and I'm the only source of it — so ask me anything." [@1:00:16–1:01:21]
- **The 3-question decision frame (the yes-ladder):** "Want me to walk you through how to decide? (1) Do you believe this gets you closer to your goal — yes/no? (2) Do you trust me to do what I said I'd do? (3) — the big one — do you believe it'll work *for you*? [if no → 'why not?' and they defend it, 6-inch putt]. Yes/yes/yes → then it's just: do you have, or have access to, what it takes to start?" [@1:01:21–1:03:00]
- **Informed-decision-requires-trial → guarantee:** "You can't make a truly *informed* decision from the outside. So I'm not asking you to decide now — I'm asking you to make an informed decision, which you can only do from the inside. If in 30 days I haven't done what I said, you get your money back." (Decide-by-not-deciding.) [@1:03:00–1:03:51]

**FUTURE frames** (magnify the cost of staying):
- **Magnify the pain of no-change:** "You've been at this 5 years. What does another 5 of the same look like — is that where you want to be? No? Then let's change it." [@1:04:50–1:05:13]
- **"Consider the options" — the risk-free logic close (needs a guarantee):** "Option 1: you do it and get the result. Option 2: you don't, and don't. Option 3: you do it and *don't* get the result — but with the guarantee, that's refunded. Every option is risk-free except one: walking out the door, which is the only path that *guarantees* you don't get there. Which risk-free option do you want?" A **logical** close, not emotional. [@1:05:13–1:06:06]
- **Do-it-now urgency:** "You'll fix this eventually, right? Then you might as well start now and enjoy the results sooner. Would you rather get there faster or slower?" [@1:06:06–1:06:32]
- **Directionally right, not perfect:** "This doesn't have to be *the* silver bullet. Does making this decision get you *closer* to your goal or further? If closer, and we keep making closer-decisions, we get there. We don't need to be snipers, just directionally right." (Defuses perfectionism.) [@1:06:32–1:07:53]

## L. The universal bonus close — "the reason not to is the reason to" (~1:07:53–1:08:44)

"The reason you're telling yourself *not* to do this is exactly the reason you *need* to. Can't afford it → that's why you can't afford to leave it broken. No time → the dependence on being un-busy is the trap. Too dependent on your partner's yes → that's the chain to break. Whatever you're casting your power to is the biggest chain you're holding; break it and the power comes back to you." Works on almost any residual objection. [hormozi-4hr @1:07:53–1:08:44]

## M. Closing philosophy — reinforces the principles layer (~1:09:00–1:15:00)

- **"You are the source. Superman isn't coming."** Every decision is a vote toward or against the person you want to become. [@1:12:23–1:12:50]
- **Buy time by buying others' mistakes.** Investing in skills/education is buying the lessons that took others years — the only way to buy time. [@1:12:50–1:13:16]
- **The time-tax of ignorance.** "Not knowing how to make $X/year is costing you $X/year." The most expensive tax is the time-tax of ignorance; pay it down by investing in experience that builds skills. [@1:13:16–1:14:42]
- **Fortunes:** built by taking a lot of risk with a little money; maintained by taking a little risk with a lot. Self-made people took the first kind. [@1:09:00–1:09:43]

## N. Delivery > content — the natural bar (~1:03:xx and ~1:29:00–1:31:00 range)

Same objection handled two ways lands completely differently. "I need to think about it" → "what are your main concerns?" (curious, resolving) reads as helpful; the same intent delivered pushily reads as "this guy's a douchebag." If the salesperson talks more than the prospect, or isn't *resolving* the specific concern, that's the failure. The go-to for a stall is a genuine **"totally — what's your main concern? let's think about it together."** [hormozi-4hr @1:03:xx, delivery section] — this is the anchor for keeping every re-voiced overcome inside NexusPoint's natural/anti-slop bar.

## I. Other frames worth citing

- **10 / 80 / 10:** ~10% never buy, ~10% always buy (don't screw it up — "just take the card"), and selling exists for the **middle 80%**. [@11:02–11:55]
- **Red Zone stat (why closing matters most):** top-5 NFL red-zone offenses made the playoffs ~90% of the time over 5 years despite other weaknesses — closing ability covers many deficiencies. [@10:03–11:02]
- **"One decision away."** Prospects (and reps) give power to sources outside their control ("blame"); the reframe is that one decision changes everything. [@11:55–13:11]
- **Understand, don't memorize** (Feynman/Munger framing): learn the *principle* behind each objection, not a script. This is why the upgrade stores a **taxonomy**, not more canned lines. [@13:11–14:00, @20:27–20:51]
