Every week, I watch B2B teams pour hours into cold email campaigns that never get replies.

They A/B test subject lines, add custom intro lines, run copy through AI optimization tools, and build elaborate multi-touch sequences. Then they send it off to thousands of prospects and wonder why reply rates hover below 1%.

The problem isn't the targeting or the timing. It's the email itself. And there's a brutally simple way to catch the issue before you send a single message.

Read it out loud.

If your email doesn't sound like something one real person would say to another, it isn't going to work. That's the test. That's the whole thing.

## Why Cold Emails Sound Like Marketing Copy

Most cold emails fail before your prospect finishes reading the first sentence. Not because of spam filters or bad subject lines—because the email sounds exactly like what it is: a template.

The giveaways are clear.

- GO PRO
- I hope this email finds you well
- I wanted to reach out because I noticed...
- We help companies like yours leverage [solution] to drive [outcome]

These phrases don't exist in actual human conversation; no one talks this way. So when prospects see them, their brains immediately categorize it as noise and they move on.

The reality is that most tools and templates designed to help you write better emails are actually teaching you to write worse cold emails. They optimize for things that feel professional—complete sentences, thorough explanations, clear value propositions—at the expense of the one thing that actually drives replies: sounding like a real person.

## The 100-Word Rule

The best-performing emails I've run across dozens of B2B industries fall under 100 words. Not because short is a hack, but because when you're forced to write under 100 words, you have to cut anything that doesn't matter.

No pleasantries. No company background. No feature list. No three paragraphs explaining the problem you solve. Just your point.

One of my clients in commercial real estate sends emails that are four sentences long. The entire pitch: I have a buyer interested in your property at this specific address, here's what they're looking for, would you consider an offer? That campaign sees an almost 4% reply rate, with nearly 40% being positive. Four sentences.

The length isn't the secret. The discipline that produces the length is the secret.

## Find the Undeniable Thing

Short emails only work if they lead with something that demands attention. I call this the undeniable thing: the one element a prospect cannot dismiss because it's too specific, too relevant, or too concrete to ignore.
