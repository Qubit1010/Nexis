# Discovery Call Script — 30-Min Ops Teardown (Source-Backed)

> **Source basis:** Cited directly from `references/research-synthesis.md` (Q4 sections 1, 5, 7, 8). The 4-Act structure pulls from the synthesized "Advanced Conversational Architectures in High-Ticket Closing" — combining Sandler Submarine, SPIN selling, Voss tactical empathy, Cole Gordon's 8-step relief ladder + 4-step objection handling, Hormozi's value equation, and Frank Kern's risk reversal.

## The frame: it's a TEARDOWN, not a sales call

**The mental model from the cold DM through the call's open:**
> "I'm going to ask 6 questions about your ops, then either show you what I'd build or tell you it's not a fit."

This positioning shift (diagnostician, not vendor) is the single most important move. It changes the prospect's posture from defensive to collaborative.

---

## Pre-Call: The Diagnostic Typeform

**Sourced principle (Hormozi):** "Do not sell deliverables; sell the diagnosis."

Send a 5-question pre-call form before every call. By the time they arrive, you have hypothesis-mode authority.

**The 5 questions:**

1. What's the most manual / time-eating part of your week right now?
2. What stack is that running through? (e.g., HubSpot + Shopify + Slack)
3. Roughly how many hours per week does it eat?
4. What have you tried to fix it — and why didn't it stick?
5. If it was solved tomorrow, what would that unlock for the business?

**Setup:** Build this as a Typeform (or Google Form). Send the link with the calendar invite confirmation. ~80% of prospects fill it out (CreatorFlow benchmark for pre-call forms).

When they don't fill it out, ask the same 5 questions in Act II.

---

## The 30-Minute Structure: 4 Acts

| Act | Time | Goal |
|---|---|---|
| **Act I** | 0-3 min | Up-Front Contract (UFC) — set frame, control, permission to say no |
| **Act II** | 3-18 min | Diagnostic Scoping + Sandler Pain Funnel |
| **Act III** | 18-23 min | Pivot + Trust-in-Method (SPIN Need-Payoff + Mistake ABC) |
| **Act IV** | 23-30 min | Close: Pre-empt objections, Double Tie-Down, Price, Silence |

Time guidelines are not rigid — but a call that's 25 minutes into Act II and hasn't gotten to Act III means you've lost discipline.

---

## ACT I — Up-Front Contract (0-3 min)

**Sourced from Cole Gordon's documented UFC frame.**

The UFC covers: **Purpose, Prospect's Agenda, Seller's Agenda, Time constraints, Outcome — including explicit permission to say "no."**

### The opening script (sourced verbatim from Cole Gordon):

> "Thank you for joining the discussion today. To ensure we respect our schedules, let's keep to the thirty minutes we blocked out. **Is now a bad time to focus on this?**"

[Wait for response. They almost always say "no, now is good."]

> "Great. The way we usually structure this is pretty straightforward. I reviewed your diagnostic [Typeform / form responses]. I want to spend the next fifteen minutes asking some targeted questions about your current [ops setup / workflow / specific area] to see if we can identify the bottleneck. If we find it, I will lay out a customized [automation blueprint / build plan] right here on the call. If at the end of this we both feel there is a strong fit, we can discuss our core engagement — which starts at $[price anchor]. If there is no fit, let's agree to say so and wrap things up cleanly. **Does that seem like a bad idea?**"

**Two no-oriented questions in the opener.** Both invite "no" — both make the prospect feel safe and in control. The price anchor mentioned in Act I prevents a price-reveal shock in Act IV.

### Aleem's adapted version:

> "Thanks for jumping on. Let's stick to the 30 min you blocked. **Is now a bad time to focus on this?**"

[Wait for yes/no.]

> "Cool. Here's how this works. I went through your form — got a picture of where the manual stuff is hitting you. Spending the first 15 min asking sharp questions to pin down THE actual bottleneck, not just symptoms. Then I'll show you the exact first automation I'd build for you, walk through what it would do, how long it takes, what it costs. Engagements run $[X-Y] depending on scope. If by the end we both feel it's a fit, we can talk next steps. If not, let's say so cleanly. **Does that seem like a bad idea?**"

## ACT II — Diagnostic Scoping + Sandler Pain Funnel (3-18 min)

**Sourced from Q5 — the 8-step Sandler Pain Funnel, verbatim.**

After the prospect describes their main pain (from form or your opening question), funnel them through these 8 questions IN ORDER. Each one digs deeper. The discipline is to NOT skip steps.

### The Sandler Pain Funnel (8 questions, sourced verbatim):

1. **"Tell me more about that."**
2. **"Can you be more specific, give me an example."**
3. **"How long has that been a problem?"**
4. **"What have you tried to do about it?"**
5. **"Did that work?"**
6. **"How much do you think this has cost you?"**
7. **"How do you feel about that?"**
8. **"Have you given up trying to fix it?"**

**Why this order matters (sourced):**
- Steps 1-2: get from surface complaint to concrete example
- Steps 3-5: surface previous failed attempts (sets up your Mistake ABC pivot later)
- Steps 6-7: convert cost from abstract to QUANTIFIED ($ and emotional)
- Step 8: psychologically commits them to wanting a fix

Between each question, **LABEL** what they just said using Voss labels (`frameworks/voss-calibrated-questions.md`). Examples:

- After Q1: "Sounds like that's quietly draining a chunk of your week."
- After Q5: "It seems like every fix has come with its own friction."
- After Q6: "That's a real number — every month this runs is essentially another headcount's worth of cost."
- After Q7: "It sounds like this is weighing on you more than just the operational side."

**By the end of Act II, the prospect has done four things:**
1. Quantified the cost in their own words
2. Admitted previous attempts failed
3. Confirmed the emotional weight
4. Established that they HAVEN'T given up — which makes them open to your solution

## ACT III — Pivot + Trust-in-Method (18-23 min)

**Sourced from Q4: SPIN Need-Payoff + Cole Gordon's "Mistake ABC" framework.**

This is where you transition from diagnosis to solution. Two sourced moves:

### Move 1: SPIN Need-Payoff Question

Before you describe ANYTHING, have THEM articulate the value of a solution.

> "If we could take [the specific manual thing they described] off your plate completely — what would that change for the business?"

[Listen. They sell themselves on it.]

> "And if that went live in, say, 14 days vs the 6-month tech project this usually feels like — what would that be worth to you?"

[They tell you it's worth a lot. In their own words. Documented as 4x more persuasive than you saying it (Rackham, SPIN Selling).]

### Move 2: Mistake ABC

**Sourced from Cole Gordon (verbatim structure):**

> "Before I show you what I'd build, let me name the mistake I see [their role] make in this area, because it's why most attempts at this fail:
>
> The biggest mistake people make when they try to fix [the specific pain] is [the specific mistake — e.g., 'they pick a tool first instead of mapping the actual workflow, so they end up automating the wrong thing']. The problem with that is [consequence — e.g., 'six weeks in, they realize they automated a process that should've been killed entirely, and they've spent $15k on something that adds friction instead of removing it'].
>
> Instead, what we do is [your specific approach — e.g., 'we spend the first 3 days mapping every step manually, then we KILL 30-40% of the steps entirely before we automate anything. The build is faster because we're automating less, and what's left actually sticks because it matches how your team actually works'].
>
> That's how we kept [Peer Name] running [specific result] for [duration] without it slipping back."

**This single move accomplishes:**
- Differentiates you from vendors they've tried
- Builds methodology trust (not feature trust — which is weak)
- Pre-empts the "we tried this before" objection
- Sets up the close with a named peer proof

## ACT IV — Close (23-30 min)

**Sourced from Q4 section 7 (price presentation) and Q4 section 6 (Cole Gordon's 4-step objection handling).**

### Move 1: Outline the integration plan (3 steps max)

> "Here's how this would actually run:
>
> Week 1: We map your stack + workflow on a Loom screen-share. I send the full audit + the exact build plan within 48 hours.
>
> Weeks 2-3: We build it. You don't touch it. We integrate, test, deploy.
>
> Week 4+: Live and saving you time. We monitor for 30 days, fix anything that breaks, hand off documentation."

**Three steps is sourced as the magic number for implementation plans on a sales call. Five+ steps overwhelms.**

### Move 2: The Double Tie-Down (Cole Gordon's verbatim move)

Before quoting price, isolate any remaining uncertainty:

> **"That aside, how do you feel about the process? Do you feel like this is what you need?"**

[Wait for affirmative.]

If they hesitate or say "I mean, it sounds good but..." — that's an uncertainty objection. Pull from `frameworks/objection-riffs.md` and resolve before moving on.

If they say "yeah, that's what we need" — proceed to price.

### Move 3: The Price Drop (sourced sequence)

**Step 3a — Set the implementation baseline first:**

> "Before we get into investment - I want to make sure we're on the same page about what this would do for the business."

[Pull up the cost they named in Act II.]

> "You said this manual stuff is costing roughly [their number] per month in lost time / wages / opportunity. So when we look at the investment, we're looking at it against that number, not against zero."

**Step 3b — Two-tier choice (Hormozi-style):**

> "Two ways to do this:
>
> Tier 1 — Done-With-You. I send you the full build playbook, host a 2-hour Loom walkthrough, you implement with your team. $[X — anchor at 25-40% of Tier 2]. Best for teams that have engineering bandwidth.
>
> Tier 2 — Done-For-You. We build, deploy, monitor for 30 days. Live in 14 days. $[Y]. Best for founders who want it off their plate fully."

**Step 3c — Tie price to value, then go silent:**

> "Tier 2 at $[Y] against the $[their cost number] you're already losing every month — pays for itself in [N] weeks. After that, it's just compounding savings."

**Then SHUT UP.** Voss principle: silence transfers commitment. Do not speak again until the prospect responds. The first one to speak loses.

### Move 4: Risk Reversal Stack (only if they hesitate)

If they don't immediately bite, layer Frank Kern's risk reversal:

> "Two guarantees:
>
> One — built and live in 14 days or you don't pay until it is.
>
> Two — if at 30 days post-launch we haven't taken at least 5 hours per week off your plate, full refund. You keep what we built.
>
> So your downside is 45 minutes of onboarding. Upside is 10+ hours/week back, forever."

### Move 5: The No-Oriented Close

Sourced from Voss as the highest-converting commitment question:

> **"Would it be a bad idea to start with Tier 2 next Monday?"**

OR if they're leaning Tier 1:

> **"Does kicking off Tier 1 by end of this week seem like a bad idea?"**

If they say "no, that doesn't seem like a bad idea" — you have your yes.

If they raise an objection — pull from `frameworks/objection-riffs.md`.

### Move 6: Pre-emptive logistics question

Per Voss's documented closing sequence:

> **"Would it offend you if I asked a few quick questions to align our implementation timeline?"**

[Soft yes. Then capture: start date, who else needs to be looped in, payment method, where the contract should go.]

## After the call: Same-Day Send-Through

**Sourced principle (Cole Gordon):** Send the contract or proposal within 2 hours of the call ending, while pain is still fresh.

The send-through includes:
1. The build plan you described, written out (1-2 pages)
2. The pricing (matches what you said on the call)
3. The two guarantees in writing
4. A start-date proposal (specific calendar slot)
5. Signature link (DocuSign / Stripe payment link)

Email subject line (sourced as high-converting):
> "Your [Specific Workflow] build plan + start date"

Body:
> "[Name] — as promised, here's the build plan and investment summary from our call.
>
> [Attached: 1-page plan PDF]
>
> Start date proposal: Monday, [specific date]. If that works, sign here: [link]. If you need to move it, just reply with which week.
>
> Recap of the guarantees:
> - Built and live in 14 days or you don't pay until live.
> - 5+ hrs/week saved within 30 days post-launch or full refund.
>
> Ready when you are.
>
> - Aleem"

## Common Failure Modes (avoid these)

| Failure | Why it kills the deal |
|---|---|
| Skipping the UFC | Prospect controls the call, you end up "pitching" |
| Skipping Pain Funnel steps | Cost never gets quantified — price feels expensive |
| Pitching before Mistake ABC | Sounds like every other vendor |
| Talking after the price drop | Voss: first to speak loses. Hold silence at least 10 seconds. |
| Single-tier price | "Yes/no" is harder than "Tier 1 or Tier 2" |
| No risk reversal | Perceived Likelihood stays low |
| Not sending the contract same day | 50% drop in close rate per Cole Gordon |
| Yes-oriented close ("Want to move forward?") | Replace with no-oriented ("Does this seem like a bad idea?") |
