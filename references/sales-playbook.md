# NexusPoint Sales Playbook

**What this is:** The strategy layer for LinkedIn and Instagram outreach. Use it to decide when to move, when to hold, what to say on the call, how to handle pushback, and how to close.

**What this is NOT:** A DM sequence reference (see `dm-sequence-structure.md` / `touch-sequence.md`) or a phase-by-phase conversation map (see `voss-framework.md` / `conversation-playbook.md`). Those handle message crafting. This handles decision-making.

## Section 1: Pacing Rules -- When to Move, When to Hold (1 min read)

### Automated Sequences (No Reply Yet)

These are fixed. Do not override them.

| Platform | Rule |
|----------|------|
| LinkedIn | No proof before DM 3. No call ask before DM 4. |
| Instagram | No NexusPoint mention before Touch 3. No call ask in the sequence at all -- Touch 3 only asks for a DM chat, not a call. |

The sequence is a pacing scaffold, not a closing tool. The call comes from a live conversation, not the sequence.

### Live Conversations (They Replied and You're Actively Messaging)

**LinkedIn -- The 3-Exchange Rule:**
Earn one phase advance per substantive exchange. A one-word reply or "lol nice" does not count. Min 4 substantive exchanges from first reply to call ask.

Exchange 1 earns: Label
Exchange 2 earns: Proof
Exchange 3 earns: Pull (they're engaging with your proof)
Exchange 4 earns: Call ask

**Instagram -- The 5-Exchange Rule:**
Instagram has an extra Deepen phase. Min 5 exchanges. The Warm Ask ("want me to send a breakdown?") is a mandatory step -- do not skip it. The call ask only comes after they engage with the Warm Ask.

**Decision tree:**

```
They replied
  └─ Did they disclose pain?
       No  → Calibrated question (still in Open/Qualify)
       Yes → Label it. Did they confirm?
              No  → Another label or calibrated question
              Yes → Did they ask what you do or how you'd fix it?
                     No  → Deepen (Instagram) or stay in Label (LinkedIn)
                     Yes → Drop ONE proof. Did they engage?
                            No  → Let it sit. Do not follow up with another proof.
                            Yes → Pull (LinkedIn) or Warm Ask (Instagram)
                                   └─ Buying signal? → Call ask
```

**Pacing fail modes -- avoid these:**
- Premature pitch: They asked "what do you do?" and you launched into everything
- Probe stacking: Two calibrated questions in one message
- Proof dumping: Multiple results listed in one message
- Skipping Instagram's Warm Ask: Going straight from Proof to call ask

## Section 2: Pitch Decision Tree -- Pitch vs. Stay in Rapport (30 sec read)

> **The core rule:** A pitch is a formal description of your offer. On DMs, never pitch. Ever. DMs qualify; calls close. The only thing that happens in a DM is moving curiosity to a booked call. The pitch happens on the call.

| Signal They Gave | What It Means | Your Move |
|-----------------|---------------|-----------|
| Short generic reply ("nice", "interesting") | Not engaged. Sequence is doing its job. | No reply needed. Let the next automated touch run. |
| Asked "what do you actually do?" | Curiosity triggered. Not a pitch trigger -- it's a label opportunity. | Qualify and label first. Do not pitch. |
| Volunteered a specific pain point | Warm. You now have something to label and eventually proof-match. | Label it, deepen, then proof when earned. |
| Confirmed your label ("yeah, exactly") | You're in proof territory. They feel understood. | Drop ONE result from the results bank. Nothing more. |
| Asked about your process, timeline, or how something works | Pull phase. They're selling themselves. | Describe, do not pitch. Let them pull further. |
| "I'd love to see this" / "when are you free?" / "what does working with you look like?" | Full buying signal. Call ask is earned. | No-oriented call ask immediately. |
| "Not interested" | Could be real, could be a soft no. | See Section 5, Objection 6. |

## Section 3: Pushing for a Call -- When and How (1 min read)

### 3A -- The Call Ask (use one per conversation, pick the right variant)

**Classic no-oriented (LinkedIn, after 4+ substantive exchanges):**
> "Would it be off base to grab 20 minutes this week so I can show you what that pipeline looks like end-to-end?"

**Soft no-oriented (Instagram, after Warm Ask engagement):**
> "Would it be crazy to do a quick 20-min call so I can show you the full thing and see if the same shape fits your setup?"

**Loss framing (when they've disclosed urgency):**
> "It sounds like this is already costing you real time every week -- would it make sense to spend 20 minutes now instead of another month of that?"

**Peer ask (for less senior or younger founders):**
> "Is it a bad idea to hop on a quick call so we can figure out if there's even a fit before either of us goes further?"

---

### 3B -- They Said Yes

Don't ask "when are you free?" -- that's friction. Do this:

> "I have [Day] at [Time] PKT or [Day] at [Time] PKT -- which works?"

Two specific slots. No open calendar question.

**Pre-call confirmation (send 24 hours before, optional but reduces no-shows):**
> "Looking forward to tomorrow. I've been thinking about what you said about [their specific pain]. Coming with a few ideas based on that."

This confirms you actually listened, creates anticipation, and cuts no-show rate.

---

### 3C -- They Deflected

| Deflection | What it usually means | Your response |
|------------|----------------------|---------------|
| "Send me more info first" | Low urgency. Wants to look without committing. | Don't send a portfolio. Ask: "Happy to -- what's most useful, a breakdown of a similar project or a short Loom of how I'd approach your situation?" |
| "Reach out in [month]" | Real timing issue. | "Makes sense. Would it help if I flagged you around [that date] so we can pick this back up?" Gets permission, removes them from the ghost bucket. |
| No reply after call ask | Life happened. Don't chase. | Wait 5-7 days. Then: "It sounds like the timing wasn't quite right -- no problem at all. If [their pain] ever moves up the priority list, I'm easy to find." Do not follow up again after this. |
| "Not interested" | See Section 5, Objection 6. | |

## Section 4: Discovery Call Script (pre-read, 5 min)

**Goal of the call:** A committed prospect who wants a proposal. Not a closed deal. Not a price discussion. A proposal commitment with a review timeline.

**Total time: 20 minutes. Five phases.**

---

### Phase 1 -- Open (2-3 min)

Never open with a pitch, credentials, or "let me tell you about NexusPoint." Open with their situation.

> "Before I get into anything on my end -- you mentioned [thing they said in DMs]. I want to make sure I actually understand that before I start talking about anything else. What does that look like day-to-day for you?"

Why: They agreed to the call -- they're already warm. Opening with their situation signals you listened and immediately separates you from every other agency call they've been on.

---

### Phase 2 -- Discovery (8-10 min)

Rotate through these 5 questions. Don't ask all 5 in order -- follow the conversation. After each answer, label before you question.

1. > "What's costing you the most time right now -- not the most annoying, the most expensive in hours?"

   Forces them to rank, not just list.

2. > "If you could fix just one thing in the next 90 days -- one process that would genuinely change how the business runs -- what would that be?"

   Hormozi dream outcome question in disguise. Gets them to name the priority without you naming it.

3. > "What have you already tried to solve this? What didn't work and why?"

   Surfaces past agency burns, DIY attempts, tool failures. Tells you what objections are coming and which proof to lead with.

4. > "How does this affect the rest of the team when it's not working?"

   Uncovers hidden stakeholders and urgency multipliers.

5. > "What would the business look like if this was just... handled -- if you didn't have to think about it?"

   Dream outcome in their words. This becomes your proposal language. Let them paint the picture.

**Label rule:** After each answer, label before asking next question.
Example: "It sounds like the real cost isn't the time itself -- it's that this bottleneck is slowing down everything downstream." Then ask the next question.

---

### Phase 3 -- Position (3-4 min)

Connect their pain to ONE proof item. Use their exact words from Discovery. No service listing, no tech stack explanation, no NexusPoint origin story.

Formula (Hormozi "so that" chain):

> "Based on what you described -- [mirror their exact words] -- here's what I'd actually build for you. [One-sentence system description], so that [their named bottleneck] stops being manual, so that [their dream outcome from Phase 2]."

Example:
> "Based on what you said about onboarding eating the same hours for every new client -- what I'd build is a client intake and setup pipeline that triggers automatically when a deal closes, so your team stops being the connective tissue between the tools, so you can onboard two clients a week instead of one."

Then drop the matched proof:
> "Built this for a similar agency. Cut their manual onboarding work by 70%."

Stop there. Do not list more services. Do not explain the tech stack. Let them ask.

---

### Phase 4 -- Qualify Budget and Fit (2-3 min)

Never skip this. Build a proposal for an unqualified prospect and you waste 3 hours.

Ask all three:

1. > "Is there a budget range you're working with for something like this, or are you more at the stage of figuring out if it's worth investing in at all?"

   No-oriented framing. Gives two honest paths. Surfaces budget without triggering defensiveness.

2. > "Is this something you'd move on in the next 30 days, or is this more of a later-in-the-year conversation?"

   Urgency filter. If they say later -- you have a nurture situation, not a close. Adjust accordingly.

3. > "Are you the one who'd be making this call, or does it need to go through someone else?"

   If there's another decision-maker, find out now -- not after sending the proposal.

---

### Phase 5 -- Close the Call, Open the Next Step (2 min)

Don't pitch pricing unless they explicitly push. Goal is proposal commitment with a timeline.

**Standard close:**
> "Based on everything you've shared, I have a clear picture of what would move the needle for [their company]. I'd like to put together a specific proposal -- not a generic one -- based on this conversation. If I send it over by [day], can you realistically review it by [end of that week]?"

Why the timeline question: Forces a commitment. If they can't commit to reviewing it, that's a signal about urgency.

**If they push for price now:**
> "Happy to give you a range. Projects in this shape typically run between $X and $Y -- the exact number depends on the scope we land on in the proposal. Does that range work for where you're at?"

Never give a single number before a signed scope. Give a range. Anchor the top end first.

**End every call with an accusation audit:**
> "Is there anything about this conversation that made you less confident in whether this is worth exploring? I'd rather know now than after you've read the proposal."

Surfaces hidden objections before they ghost you.

## Section 5: Objection Handling in DMs (30 sec to find, 1 min to read)

### Objection 1: "I don't have the budget for this right now."

**What it usually means:** One of three things -- genuinely can't afford it (wrong prospect), hasn't seen enough value yet (pitch timing problem), or using budget as a soft no (not sold).

**Response:**
> "It sounds like the timing might not be right -- is it the budget that's the real constraint, or is it more that you haven't seen something specific enough to know if it's worth moving on?"

If it's a real budget constraint:
> "That makes sense. Most of what I build for teams at your stage started as a smaller pilot -- if we were to scope just the highest-pain piece, what would that be?"

Scope reduction, not discount.

---

### Objection 2: "I need to think about it."

**What it usually means:** You moved too fast, or they haven't connected your proof to their specific pain. "Think about it" is almost always a trust deficit, not a time deficit.

**Response:**
> "That makes total sense -- it sounds like you haven't quite seen how this maps to your specific situation yet. What's the part you'd want to think through most?"

Label their actual hesitation, then surface it with a calibrated question.

---

### Objection 3: "We already have someone handling this." / "We already use [tool]."

**What it usually means:** Either genuinely covered (wrong prospect), or what they have isn't working as well as they think.

**Response:**
> "It sounds like you've already got something in place -- how's that working for you right now?"

If they say "great," thank them and exit gracefully. If their answer is anything less than enthusiastic, that's your crack. Stay curious.

---

### Objection 4: "Send me your portfolio / more info."

**What it usually means:** Low urgency. Wants to look without committing. A portfolio dump will not move this forward.

**Response:**
> "Happy to -- what would be most useful: a breakdown of a specific project that matches what you're working on, or a short Loom of how I'd approach your situation?"

Micro-commitment. Forces them to engage rather than passively receive. Whichever they pick tells you what they actually need.

---

### Objection 5: "AI automation isn't really relevant for us." / "We're not ready for that."

**What it usually means:** They're picturing something complex and expensive, or they think AI automation is for tech companies.

**Response:**
> "It sounds like you're picturing something more complex than what I typically build -- what specifically feels like it's not ready on your end?"

Label the fear, then surface what it actually is. If it's a complexity concern, use a low-complexity proof item (client onboarding pipeline, email + CRM workflow) to reframe it.

---

### Objection 6: They went silent after showing real interest.

**What it usually means:** Life happened. Or they got distracted. Chasing with "just checking in" is the worst possible move.

**Wait 5-7 days, then:**
> "It sounds like things got busy -- no worries. Wanted to pass along one thing I thought was relevant to [their specific pain] before I close out my notes on this. [One concrete, genuinely useful observation -- not a pitch.] Curious if it resonates."

**If still no reply (wait another 5-7 days):**
> "Have you decided to move in a different direction on this? Happy either way -- just want to close my notes out."

No-oriented, forces a reply, removes you from the "awkward follow-up" category. Almost always triggers a response.

## Section 6: Prospect-Type Scripts (1 min per type)

### Type 1: Solo Founder / Early-Stage (1-5 people)

**Who they are:** Doing everything themselves. Every manual hour is an hour not on product or clients. Fear of wasted money -- can't afford a mistake.

**Discovery opener:**
> "What's the thing eating your week that you'd automate tomorrow if you could?"

**Label that lands:**
> "It sounds like you're still the connective tissue between everything -- the tool holding the stack together."

**Best proof match:** Client onboarding pipeline (70% manual work reduction) or AI content engine.

**Call pitch angle:** Frame as ROI, not cost.
> "This isn't an agency project -- it's buying back 10 hours a week. At your rate, what's 10 hours worth to you?"

---

### Type 2: Ops Lead at a Scaling Company (20-80 people)

**Who they are:** Their job is making the company run faster without adding headcount. They've been burned by agencies before. They want systems, not projects. They speak in processes, handoffs, and throughput.

**Discovery opener:**
> "What's the part of your operation that's breaking most visibly as the team grows?"

**Label that lands:**
> "It seems like the handoff layer is where things fall through -- the space between tools where nobody fully owns the data flow."

**Best proof match:** Lead-to-outreach pipeline (80% follow-up time reduction, 3 handoffs eliminated).

**Call pitch angle:** Frame as infrastructure, not a feature.
> "What I build isn't a project -- it's the layer your team stops having to be. Minimal ongoing overhead, runs without them."

---

### Type 3: E-Commerce Founder (Shopify, direct-to-consumer, 5-30 people)

**Who they are:** Revenue-obsessed. Every manual process is a leak. They have Shopify chaos -- reconciliation, order management, customer follow-up, inventory -- all partially automated but not connected.

**Discovery opener:**
> "What's the part of your Shopify setup that's still manual that drives you the most crazy every week?"

**Label that lands:**
> "It looks like the tools are there but none of them are actually talking to each other -- so the seams are still being managed by a person."

**Best proof match:** Multi-step e-commerce automation (Shopify + HubSpot + Slack, 3 handoffs removed).

**Call pitch angle:** Hormozi "so that" chain connecting their specific tools.
> "We connect [their tools] so that [specific process] runs automatically so that you get back [X hours/week] so that [their named outcome]."

---

### Type 4: SaaS Founder (B2B, pre-PMF or early growth)

**Who they are:** They understand automation conceptually. Their pain is usually in sales pipeline management, lead qualification, or customer onboarding -- the places where manual work is slowing GTM.

**Discovery opener:**
> "What does your sales follow-up process look like right now -- fully systematized or still pretty manual?"

**Label that lands:**
> "It sounds like the actual sales motion is solid but the work around it -- qualifying, following up, logging -- is still eating the team's time."

**Best proof match:** AI lead gen pipeline (raw data in, scored and enriched prospects out).

**Call pitch angle:** Connect automation to revenue acceleration, not ops efficiency.
> "Every hour your team spends on data hygiene is an hour not in front of a buyer. That's what we automate."

---

### Type 5: Service Business / Agency (marketing, legal, consulting, 5-25 people)

**Who they are:** Billing time for work. Their efficiency problem is their profit problem. Repeatable deliverables still done manually every time: client onboarding, reporting, project updates.

**Discovery opener:**
> "How much of your team's time is going into the stuff around the client work vs. the actual client work itself?"

**Label that lands:**
> "It sounds like the margin leak is on the operational side -- the reporting, onboarding, handoffs -- not the delivery itself."

**Best proof match:** Automated client onboarding pipeline (70% manual reduction) or AI email + CRM workflow (hours to minutes response time).

**Call pitch angle:** Frame in billable hours recovered.
> "If we cut the admin overhead in half, what does that translate to in capacity to take on another client without adding headcount?"

## Section 7: Closing Language (30 sec read)

### 7A -- Verbal Commitment on the Call

Before hanging up, get a review timeline -- not just a "sounds good."

> "Before we close out -- I want to make sure I build this around the right thing. If I send the proposal by [day], can you realistically review it by [end of that week]?"

If they can't commit to a review timeline, that tells you something important about urgency.

---

### 7B -- Proposal Follow-Up (send 48 hours after delivery)

Accusation audit disguised as a check-in. Not "what did you think?" Not "ready to move forward?"

> "Hey [Name], wanted to check in on the proposal -- not to push, but genuinely curious if anything in there didn't land the way you'd expect. If the framing was off on something, I'd rather know and fix it than have you make a decision based on a misread."

---

### 7C -- Ghosting Recovery (7-10 days after proposal, no reply)

> "Have you decided to go in a different direction on this? No pressure either way -- I just want to close my notes out on my end."

Classic Voss no-oriented ghost trigger. Almost always gets a reply.

---

### 7D -- The Close Itself (when they say yes verbally)

Move immediately. Do not wait. The gap between verbal yes and signed paperwork is where deals dissolve.

> "Great. I'll send you the agreement and invoice today. Once that's signed, I'll schedule the kickoff call -- usually within 48 hours. What's the best email to send the paperwork to?"

## Section 8: Voss Quick-Reference (10 sec read)

| Technique | When to use | Exact starter |
|-----------|------------|---------------|
| Label | They disclosed emotion, pain, or hesitation | "It sounds like..." / "It seems like..." / "It looks like..." |
| Calibrated question | Need more info without triggering defensiveness | "What's..." / "How are you..." (never "why" -- sounds like an accusation) |
| Mirror | They said something important -- get them to elaborate | Repeat last 2-3 words with a slight upward tone |
| No-oriented CTA | Any call ask or commitment request | "Would it be off base..." / "Is it a bad idea..." / "Would it be crazy..." |
| Accusation audit | Opening a call or pre-empting an objection | "I know you're probably thinking..." / "You might be wondering..." |
| Rule of Three | Locking commitment before ending the call | Get them to confirm the same decision three different ways |
| Loss framing | Urgency without fake scarcity | "Every [time unit] without this means [real cost]..." |
| Ghosting trigger | They went completely silent | "Have you decided to go in a different direction?" |
